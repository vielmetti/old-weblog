17 December 2014

Dr. Dobb's Journal shuffles off this mortal coil

Dr. Dobb's Journal is closing down, with its last issue appearing at the end of 2014. They tell their story in a final report.

The key insight into the demise of this 38 year old publication is that this was their best year for online web traffic, yet their revenues were heading straight down.

Four years ago, when I came to Dr. Dobb's, we had healthy profits and revenue, almost all of it from advertising. Despite our excellent growth on the editorial side, our revenue declined such that today it's barely 30% of what it was when I started.

My memory of reading Dr. Dobb's dates back to the early 1990s, when it was a source for careful writing (complete with source code) on programming issues of the day. Now days there's an infinite supply of technical writing on the net, so it's not surprising that ad revenues dried up (or more pointedly got concentrated on Google the gatekeeper for much of online traffic).

"Running light without overbyte" was their first slogan. You can pick up a first edition of their compiled first year of publication (1976) from Kuenzig Books.

Some notes on Echolink and websdr

Echolink is a system that lets amateur radio operators transmit from their computer, through a relay at a suitable Echolink server. You have to be a licensed ham to get on the network, and typically there's a two-way repeater set up to some radio station so that you can hear what's being said on RF and vice versa.

websdr is a system that lets anyone listen to a slice of radio spectrum. There are websdr servers running around the globe, each tuned in to some particular slice of the radio waves. Multiple people can tune in at the same time and hear their own piece of the airwaves.

The interesting thing happens when you connect these two systems together. Some small number of the websdr receivers tune into the 2 meter band, which is the typical band used by Echolink. Using Echolink (or in my case the Mac application Echomac) I identified an Echolink host in the vicinity of the websdr receiver, then connected to it. Sure enough, I was able to hear my call sign back in through the websdr front end.

This little image shows the result. Transmitted via DM0ZHS, and monitored at http://129.187.5.188:8901/ on 145237.50 kHz, I'm sending from Isen, Germany and receiving the transmission at the Olympic Tower in Munich, Germany.

Obituary: John Gall, 1925-2014

Gall, John C. 9/18/1925 - 12/15/2014 Walker, MN John C. Gall, Jr., beloved husband, father, pediatrician, scholar and author, passed away with his wife by his side Dec. 15, 2014, in Walker, MN, from natural causes. He was 89. John was born on September 18, 1925 in Washington, D.C. and grew up near Upperville, VA. In 1945 he was drafted into the Army Air Corps and sent to Germany, where he served as a medic in the Allied army of occupation. During his tour of duty he attended the Nuremberg trials, which kindled in him a lifelong passion to heal the afflicted and promote justice. Upon returning home he furthered his education, graduating from Yale and George Washington University Medical School. He was a postdoctorate fellow at the Mayo Clinic, where he was dubbed “the best pediatric diagnostician [they had] ever seen!” In addition he earned a certification in Human Genetics and completed 7 years of therapy training. Dr. Gall began his medical career as a researcher at the University of Virginia, followed by a 3-year stint at the Geisinger Clinic in Danville, PA. He next went into private practice in Miles City, MT, but was soon invited to teach at the University of Michigan Medical School in Ann Arbor, MI. There he also served as head of the Plymouth State Home, where he did research partially funded by the Kennedy family foundation, and named and described several syndromes of the developmentally disabled . In addition he established a long-running private pediatric practice of 38 years. During this period he developed a special appreciation for, and an enduring interest in, the importance of nonverbal communication between very young children and their parents. This led him to expand his practice to include support and education classes, in which he was aided by his late wife, Beth, and then by his wife Carol. Their observations and anecdotes are included in Elegant Parenting, written with wife, Beth; in Dancing with Elves written by John, and with wife Carol he wrote Hit by a Low-Flying Goose. John’s best known book is System Antics, later published under the title The Systems Bible, a humorous but insightful study of the problems and pitfalls that afflict every type of system that human beings create-whether mechanical, economic, corporate, aeronautical, legal, linguistic, religious, medical or electronic. It was first published in 1976 by the New York Times/Quadrangle Press and has been translated (officially or unofficially) into at least 14 languages. He lectured widely throughout the United States and was interviewed by the Voice of America on nuclear proliferation issues after its publication. John and Carol were both invited to speak in London in 2012 to a group of CEOs and other executives from around the world and John was introduced as “The Grandfather of Systems Theory.” He retired to Walker, MN, in 2001 in search of privacy and uninterrupted time, which he used to write seven other books, including a novel, First Queen, plus books and video books on parenting and the art of pediatric practice. He was very proud that the great Swiss psychiatrist, Alice Miller, who introduced the world to the subject of child sexual abuse, endorsed his work before she died. His work has also been endorsed by others too numerous to name, but including such luminaries as the head of the Dept. of Psychology at Cambridge, University, who forwarded his material to several British social service agencies. In the late 1990s John helped to write the National Pediatric Nurse Practitioner Exam in Washington D.C several years running. More recently he was invited to Washington D.C. by a government psychiatrist and consulted with this man on the development of psychological profiles of moles (double agents). He also spoke at a symposium at Princeton University entitled “Children under the Shadow of War.” The group included scholars from around the world, and filmmakers who had photographed kidnapped children who had been conscripted into war and forced to commit atrocities. It was John’s job to deliver the final speech, summarizing and tying together all of the presentations. This was the only lecture open to the public. John gave a moving presentation that left not a dry eye in the audience. In the final months of his life, John continued to think and write actively on a wide range of subjects. Most recently he completed a 4th edition of Systemantics and contributed, with other international scholars, to a book on Panarchy, due to be published in 2015. John was preceded in death by his first wife, Beth, and three step-grandsons, Michael Nelson, and Franklin and Alexander Sawvel. His is survived by his wife, Carol, brothers Dr. Joseph Gall (Diane Dwyer) and Howard, sons Duane (Valerie) and David (Suzette), step-sons Steven (Sarah) and Terrell (Eleanor) Nelson, and grandchildren Izzie, Nathan, Sebastian, and Simone, plus step-grandchildren Anthony, Cody, Alaya, Nicole, and Branden Nelson. A private family ceremony will be held in the Episcopal Church in Upperville, VA, in the late spring. -

Establishing a routine for processing inbound communications

I used Google Inbox for a little while, but gave up on it. It turns out that the routine I have with Google's Gmail for handling inbound correspondence is pretty good, and Inbox was too different from Gmail to make it worthwhile to do an easy transition.

I don't know if my workflow for handling message traffic is perfect, but it seems to be pretty good. Email comes in, I read it, and anything that deserves further attention but can't be handled right away is starred. The next round of handling works its way through the starred messages, and when something in that queue is stuck and needs more attention than I can get to at the moment it goes into a Github issue. Periodically I work through Github, sort out some priority items, and try to crank through the queues.

The problem with systems is that they periodically fail. My system works OK as well as it goes, but it can fall down when I get a whole series of messages from one person right one after the other. I'm accustomed to a routine where in most cases there's one or two things outstanding that need to be resolved on any ongoing conversation, and the burst of unanswered email gets in the way of that effort.

If you have a system that allows you to accumulate a queue, there will inevitably be delays. I like being absolutely on top of my inbox and able to answer queries as they come in, and there's some basic capacity in messages per hour that I can process when I'm "on". Even emptying out a queue into an issue tracker can take time, and thus inevitably there's some things that don't get done instantly.

In periodic posts to Twitter I try to track my backlog. In truth there's always something that will cause an event in my todo list to be overtaken by events, at a pace of about one or two a day. The key mostly for me is just to keep things flowing through, and to try very hard not to get so bogged down in the moment that I can't tackle tougher tasks that actually need more than 10 minutes of concentration.

24 November 2014

Github-based kanban boards: Zenhub, Waffle, Huboard, Blossom et al

A kanban board is simply a two-dimensional view of a series of work queues. By moving a piece of work from one column to another, you update its status. This gives you a quick way to visualize your current priorities, as well as making it very clear when one or more of your queues are too full.

I've been using Github issues as a personal issue tracker for a while, using its milestones feature to track categories. This has been mostly a one-dimensional exercise of managing very long lists, or shorter lists. It's been cumbersome to use either labels or milestones to handle the quick need to say to yourself "I'm going to focus on this now" without the issue sinking back quickly into a mile-long undifferentiated list.

Enter a whole series of Github add-ons that all add a similar feature, the kanban-style column view. I'll list them here with any experience I currently have using them. My best bet is that at some point Github absorbs one of these and makes it its own, but that is tempered by the realization that Github issues really isn't changing much as a product.

Zenhub, zenhub.io. This is a Google Chrome plugin, which gives you a kanban-style view of your issues. It only works in Chrome. I tried it, but didn't see an easy way to configure which columns referred to which labels, and after a few tries gave up on it.

Waffle, waffle.io. At the moment this is what I'm using. You authenticate to it with Github, and then it displays your issues. Free (for now), they are advertising that it will be $7/mo for private repos, which is what I currently pay for all of the rest of Github.

Huboard, huboard.com. Free for open source repos or $7/mo/user for private repos. 14 day free trial.

Blossom, blossom.io. No free plan, 14 day trial, minimum is a 5 user plan at $22/mo.

Task Tub, tasktub.com. Company page does not load.

SweepBoard, sweepboard.com. Free for open source projects.

When I first started finding these sorts of things, I didn't really realize that it was a whole category of products. Most of them have some other features beyond the kanban to add to Github as a project management tool. I don't think there's a category winner, but if you are interested in visualizing your work and taking your todo list beyond the simple long list this is a useful set of tools to explore.

22 November 2014

Non-traditional maps to monitor severe weather

When ordinary weather maps don't tell you what the worst of the weather is like out there, I turn to these sources for information.

  1. Twitter, especially. A search this morning for "ann arbor ice" gave plenty of illustrations why it was not suitable to be out walking around. Streets and highways were bad, and a Delta flight slid off the runway at DTW. For Michigan weather follow the #miwx hashtag.

  2. Google Maps with the "traffic" overlay. Zoom way out, and you'll see splotches of red, showing where traffic is moving at significantly lower than posted speeds; zoom in, and you get recent reports from motorists using Waze reporting specific incidents.

  3. Broadcastify's "top feeds". Here you count on citizens monitoring public safety traffic to give you an idea of what kinds of places are working a lot of incidents. The top feeds can also be set up to generate alerts on your phone if you use their mobile app.

  4. Power outage maps, for when storms and incidents get really bad. There's no one single source to go to, but if you can figure out which utility company covers an area, their outage map is a good measure of storm location or path and intensity.

  5. The morning's problem was ice. An unusual and good map of ice risk is the SPIA Index, a metric that combines an ice forecast with a wind forecast to predict damage.

How does this system even work?

Some questions to ask while you're trying to sort this out.

  1. Draw a diagram of the entire system, from one end to the other. Label the parts. Identify especially the parts that "just work", because undoubtably there are unexpected cases that won't work just when you need them to work.

  2. Identify the message flows through the system. Where do signals get generated, where do they get retransmitted, where do they get changed and when does a human interpret them? Look for feedback loops, where messages oscillate between two parts of a system.

  3. Figure out what kind of time scale you need to operate on, and understand the characteristics of delays within the system. Identify the cycle time of critical components, how often they can be queried and especially how long it takes to return an accurate reading.

  4. Form follows funding. Understand the financial flows through the system that constrain the data flows. If a process costs money, sort out the planning and budgeting implications of making a change. Systems that are subject to external regulation may have unexpected constraints on operations and reporting,

  5. Complex working systems are in a perpetual state of partial failure, and often the resolution of partial failures involves human inputs. Consider the human element in the systems design, and draw that into your functional design.

It's possible to overthink all of these, and some of the parts decompose into smaller parts that are in turn rather complex. Inside every complex working system is a simpler system that works, and you are looking to identify that at the core of your efforts.

Wake up, get a cup of coffee, start typing

Wake up, get a cup of coffee, start typing. That's a pretty typical pattern for me these days, and I'm trying to make sense of whether it's a good one.

The hardest part of making a morning typing routine work is to try to figure out what indeed it is that you are focusing on in those early caffeinated hours. There are lots of competitors for the morning attention, including yesterday's inbox, the overnight news, and the steady tick of Twitter and Facebook. You could sit and just read things for a while and not get anything in particular done.

One element of morning focus could be simply on churning out something - anything - that was a sign of progress towards a goal. This doesn't even need to be anything substantial, just a morning wakeup exercise to get the keys moving. I experimented with 750 Words, a system designed for just this daily writing exercise. That was ultimately unsatisfying, though I like the idea the details didn't work out. This effort gets called "Morning Pages" which I guess justifies as part of a writer's craft.

Daily work is hard, hard work. It asks you to be able to tune in and focus each and every day, whether or not you woke up with that idea in mind. Carving out that chunk of the day, committing to a specific goal, tracking your progress towards the goal (without the tracking being its own burden), and following through in the absence of some easy feedback.

I read my own advice about doing things frequently and sometimes I laugh at my own cheery suggestions. "Write about the same thing twice a day", I cheerily suggest in the Blogger's Secret, "and do that for six months". OK, which thing? What happens if two weeks into this commitment you discover that it's not the right thing to do?

So wake up, get a cup of coffee, start typing. Produce something. If you need to determine how it fits into a bigger plan, do your edits in the afternoon.

Tonight's #wjchat is about social media for long-term editorial projects

wjchat is a weekly Wednesday night chat session on Twitter. "wj"

in this case is short for "web journalism". Tonight's session is on using social media for long term editorial projects.

The longest long-term editorial project I'm working on that's relevant to this is Arborwiki, which is now part of the Localwiki project. It's a local encyclopedia of the Ann Arbor area, and as such it's a very useful spot to accumulate the back story for any story that you want to tell. It's an ideal place to write the little biographical sketch for someone who's in the news, to write up a brief history of important issues, or to collect public records that help you understand the ins and outs of some ongoing project. The effort started in 2005 and is still going strong.

I've gotten so accustomed to having a wiki as an outboard memory that I understand that if I don't write something down, I'm not likely to be able to find it again. That impulse is tempered by the realization that writing it down might take an appreciable amount of time, since the value of a fragment or an incomplete image is limited.

The biggest headache of any long-term editorial project is that it's very hard to get the exact right point of view expressed so that if you walk away from the thing you're working on for a year or two that you can come back to it and bring it up to date easily. The durable pages look like a "tick-tock" with a heavy emphasis on a timeline and much less in the way of explanation. If you can at least get a solid timeline nailed down, you can at least pick up the story (in non-wiki, proper story form) after leaving it and be have some good backstory to pick and choose from.

19 November 2014

When I don't write it down to share with the world I don't learn it

I'm working on a project that will remain nameless. The sponsor of the project asked me not to write about it, and I agreed. I'm spending a lot of time communicating about the project, but those communications are all private. It's intensely frustrating.

I learn best by writing about what I'm doing, in enough detail to capture some of the very small bits of what had happened while it was happening. There's no better way for me to learn - and to teach - than by documenting the bits and pieces as I go, while they are still fresh. I write in a form that's framed so that the pieces which are sharable are shared widely, and if there's anything private that gets edited out. I've learned to do this over time, and when it works, it works well.

When you write things down for the world to see, such as in this weblog or carefully for Twitter, you get the benefit of fast feedback. People can see what you are interested in, and you can reach out to them with something relevant on the front page of what they see when they look for you. If there are questions that are top of mind they can go to the top of the page. It's a very effective way to take advantage of introductions, and to reach out beyond people you know.

Without the ability to write things down it's easy to flounder. You end up reviewing ground that you had gone over before because you forgot it, and didn't write it down somewhere you could find it. Decisions that were made and observations that were agreed on get revisited sometimes endlessly because there's no contemporaneous record of the conclusion. Obscure details that you want to follow up on later get wiped out.

This particular project has an internal communications tool, which will also remain nameless. It's a stylized, formalized, cartoonish caricature of what people actually use in real life, and it's designed to maximize management visibility into the process rather than the utility of the process to the goal. In many ways it reminds me of other systems I've been forced into where the sole measure of progress was the easiest thing to measure, and all of your hard qualitative work gets squeezed down into a single number (number of transactions, queue depth) that bears very little resemblance to anything useful.

I've written before about systems and why they fail; that's actually part of my 140 character Twitter biography. Here the failures probably are on my part, for not understanding the project expectations before starting, and not being able to exit gracefully before the effort is over. The whole effort has given me a sour taste. Most importantly, I understand sadly that being asked not to write has been to cut off the most important part of what I can contribute to an effort, and in retrospect I should have walked out of the room when that happened.

19 November 2014

In retrospect, I should have walked out of the room.

Note to self: if you get asked to be part of a project, and then subsequently get asked to not talk about that project, renegotiate the terms of that effort promptly. Non-disclosure has wide ranging consequences, and that's especially true when you habitually learn about how something works by writing about it.

In retrospect, I should have walked out of the room. That would have been brave and not foolish.

That bright idea of yours might cost you $25,000

A cautionary tale.

What's the value of an idea? Consider that some ideas might have a negative value; indeed that most of them might. It may take you some substantial investment to test out your idea, to prove it, to build something minimal to show people that it was real. And then when you're ready with it there might be a net result that you finally have talked to enough people that you know that you need to scrap it and start over.

If then I am skeptical of your great new project and want to be critical of it first rather than embrace it, it's not because I don't think that you should be at all ashamed or sad to have come up with anything good. It's more for me - this is the first step of defensiveness that will help me not get involved.

Please, if I pitch something in your direction, try to dissuade me from doing it. Tell me that no one has done it, or that everyone has done it, or that there's no customers, or that it would cost too much to try, or that it can't scale, or whatever. Shake me out of my temporary enthusiasm. I can generate a lot of ideas that each might take $25,000 to prove out, and the faster I can get to "no" the happier I'll be.

Tor network takedown by the Nazgl

I would liken Tor to putting on Sauron's ring. The wearer is invisible to ordinary beings, like Men, but highly visible to the Nazgl.

— Matthew Green (@matthew_d_green) October 24, 2014

Ars Technica writes (November 9, 2014) about the takedown of many sites on the Tor network. An excerpt:

The theory posited by the kid who started Doxbin to nachash was that the attack was an attempt to force connections to the sites various .onion addresses to follow paths that went over Tor network nodes set up by law enforcement. By filling up the circuits through secure Tor network nodes, law enforcement operatives could have made it possible to connect to the services only through Tor routing servers they controlledallowing them to see the real Internet Protocol address of the server hosting them.

In general, beware any service that provides security, because any users of that service become targets.

Where do you write something that's longer than a tweet, but shorter than an essay?

Where do you write something that's longer than a tweet, but shorter than an essay?

There's a space on the net for things that you put together that don't fit into a tweet, but that also don't really require a whole long essay.

I've been writing these in my blog for years, and so I have a bit of infrastructure put together for organizing, collecting, and finding them again. A lot of people who are on Twitter don't have a blog, though, and so their fragments stay fragmentary or get lost in the mists of Twitter time.

There are plenty of places to post disconnected paragraphs online - you can stick them on Facebook (where they will be quickly seen and quickly lost), you can dump them into a Github gist (where only techies will be able to find them again), or - best of all - you can use your own web space in some blog-like time-sequenced away to push things into the world for later retrieval.

Some other people talking about this at the moment:

  • Gina Trapani: I find tweets too reductionist and Medium pieces too bloviating
  • Marco Arment: Too much of my writing in the last few years has gone exclusively into Twitter. I need to find a better balance.
  • Andy Baio: I miss seeing people I admire post stuff longer than a tweet.

It was noted by several people that Tumblr fits this pattern well; alas, I don't Tumbl very well.

Applesauce recipe

APPLESAUCE

At farmer's market, ask the farmer for a half a peck or a peck of "mixed seconds for sauce". Use whatever apples they give you. In November 2014 I paid $7 for a peck of mixed seconds at the Ann Arbor Farmers Market.

Quarter and core many apples; do not peel. Cover bottom of pot with water, put in the apples, half (or a whole) orange (pref. organic, with seeds removed), one (or two) cinnamon sticks. Cook over gentle heat for at least 20 minutes, up to several hours, until apples break down. Cool. Remove cinnamon stick, scrape flesh from orange and return to pot; discard peel. Put apple mixture through food processor; grind well, so that no large pieces of peel remain.
