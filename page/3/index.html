8 December 2015

Requesting meeting minutes under the Open Meetings Act

I’ve been frustrated in the past at the slow pace of getting meeting minutes through Freedom of Information Act requests. Queries to the City of Ann Arbor routinely get the full round of routine delays, with several cases where I’ve had to wait 15 business days to get routine meeting minutes.

As it happens, there’s an alternative to asking for minutes, using the Michigan Open Meetings Act directly as your guide. Here’s a recent request that gave me same-day results:

Hello, this is a request under the Michigan Open Meetings Act.

Please make available for inspection the draft minutes of the (named)
Committee meeting of today, December 8, 2015.

Under the terms of the Act, draft minutes are to be made available
for inspection within 8 business days of the date of the meeting,
or by my calendar December 18, 2015.

(Name) is listed as the staff contact for this board.

Thank you for your cooperation.

The request was routed to the staff member, and the minutes were prepared and ready and shared with me the same day that the meeting was held. Same day service!

4 December 2015

Split tunnels considered harmful

Previously: Considered harmful essays considered harmful, Eric Meyer, 2002; also Go To Statement Considered Harmful, CACM, 1968; also Encyclopedia of things considered harmful, from cat-v.org.

Split tunnels are a VPN construction where instead of sending all of your traffic for the network in a single encrypted tunnel that collects everything, you instead route some traffic down the encrypted link and other traffic goes in the clear through your default connection. It’s possible that you might have a tunnel configuration with multiple splits if e.g. you needed to get to private address space at two or more organizations simultaneously.

If you have a choice, don’t use a split tunnel. Here’s why.

First and foremost are security considerations. The user of the split tunnel introduces a possible forwarding path between the outside Internet world (full of nefarious intent) and the inside Internet world that you have a secure path to and its chewy center. If an attacker knows that you are using a split tunnel, they can put something on the public Internet that causes you to send their traffic directly through to your private network while you have all your trust relationships intact. This could be bad, and if your security instincts trend towards professional paranoia, you’ll understand that there’s a real risk.

A second security consideration is unplanned leakage of private information into the Internet. The split tunnel in its ordinary configuration expects that you can distinguish protected traffic from unprotected traffic by its IP address. The nature of the modern Internet is that a browser can and will connect to dozens or hundreds of hosts on its way to filling out a web page, and any one of those connections might expose or leak some information about what you were doing on the “private” link that could be of use to an adversary or snoop.

Finally, split tunnels are difficult to verify as correctly configured. The managed endpoint of the tunnel can only see traffic destined for it, and can only verify that what’s there is going to the endpoint. With a split, the managed endpoint doesn’t have visibility into all traffic destinations and thus is completely unaware of any exfiltration attempts.

However, there may be reasons that a split tunnel is unavoidable. Here are some considerations to balance against the risk.

A split tunnel is more versatile than a full tunnel. If your users need to connect to more than one administrative domain simultaenously, they may need to configure their local host setup to have two or more split tunnels running at once.

If the tunnel endpoint is at the long end of a slow connection, or if the trusted managed endpoint is bandwidth or CPU constrained, performance through a full tunnel may be unacceptably bad. Splitting the tunnel means that only necessary traffic goes through it, allowing better use of network resources.

Finally, there’s the cost. If the user is going to be a typical Internet use and do simultaneous Spotify or Netflix or other streaming media, it seems insane to pay VPN charges or engineer your own VPN endpoint to handle that full stream of data when it could very easily go over a lowest-cost unencrypted link.

2 December 2015

The productivity oscillator

I have been using Github issues for a long time as a todo list tracking and general productivity tool. It works well enough that I’ve opened over 4000 issues and have closed most of them.

The perpetual management challenge is in deciding how many issues is OK to leave open. Put too few in, and it feels like you have things to do that are not written down; put in too many, and you’re likely to have duplication and you forget what it is that you were trying to do in the first place.

The best part of the challenge is having some illusion of control over what is going on. I’m trying to work the queue this way. Some times my goal will be to create more open issues, putting things on the list to increase it up to a target goal. Other times the goal will be to reduce the size of the list by finishing tasks, clearing duplicates, or deciding not to get things done. By framing it as a deliberate game of “add tasks up to a target” or “clear tasks down to a target” I’m hoping to trick myself into taking on the easy things that I wrote down a month ago that will take a minute to do but are off my immediate radar.

The productivity oscillator suggests both a pattern of work and the illusion that whatever you are doing is the right thing to do. If you’re stuck trying to get stubborn things accomplished, write done some new easy goals and get those taken care of. It’s a quantified form of structured procrastination - which it turns out I’ve been talknig about for 10 years.

I wonder if there’s anything still on my todo list from 2005!

October 2015 food inspections for Washtenaw County

Every month or so the Washtenaw County Health Department releases the previous month’s health inspection reports. These are published to the county’s contracted web service, and a copy of those is extracted for the Food Inspections service designed by Wayne Eaker.

There are 272 inspections for October 2015 and 50 more for November 2015 available. Here are some of the low lights of this month’s round of inspections, places where you might want to think about twice before making a visit.

Dalat, Ypsilanti, inspected on November 3. “Observed sewage backing up to the floor underneath the food prep sink.” “Raw chicken and beef boxes being used to store other ready to eat foods (i.e. vegtables) in various coolers in the facility.” 12 violations total, 8 critical.

Melting Pot, Ann Arbor, their last inspection on November 2 before the restaurant closed for good later in the month. “Observed the dishwasher go from loading soiled dishes to putting away clean dishes without washing hands.”

Great Plains Burger Company, Ann Arbor, inspected on October 21 with 14 total violations. “Observed many small flies (fruit flies) around prep sink where french fries are prepared.” “Containers used to store french fries observed soiled. A cleaned inverted container observed with many flies trapped under it and were seen landing on container.”

Shalimar Restaraunt on Main Street in Ann Arbor, inspected on October 29 with 3 total violations. “Observed 4 bottles of liquor with flies inside.”

Haab’s Restaurant in Ypsilanti with 15 violations on October 21. “Observed the hand sink for the basement kitchen wrapped in plastic wrap to avoid using due to unrepair leaking. The only available hand sink is in another area through a door and significant distance away from this kitchen.” “Observed severe infestation of drain flies noted at bar and main kitchen.” “Observed drain flies in the bottom of open bottle of scotch at bar.”

Please check out your favorite destination before visiting!

First snow of the 2015 season

The first snow of the 2015 season for Ann Arbor happened today, November 21. The forecast is 6 to 10 inches.

Roads are slippery, and there are reports of a number of crashes and cars in the ditch on area freeways. I’m listening to the Washtenaw scanner on Broadcastify and it’s a steady stream of road run-offs.

Farmers Market was busy but snowy. Holiday greenery was well in evidence. We picked up smoked lake trout, cabbage, garlic, rapini, and a cookie and a donut for my market companions.

My favorite source of snow forecasting and snow reporting is NOHRSC, the National Operational Hydrologic Remote Sensing Center, a slightly contrived acronym for the National Weather Service’s comprehensive snow information service.

The start of the snow season means that I dust off the Heikki Lunta twitter account, @heikkilunta . Heikki is the Finnish-American god of snow, and his Twitter account mostly retweets people who are doing snow dances.

November has been unusually mild and sunny, so this snow brings us a little closer to normal. Time to check to be sure that every has hats and gloves and suitably durable boots and shoes.

OpenVPN in a container

A few notes on setting up OpenVPN in a container.

I’m using CoreOS, which gives me Docker 1.7.1 in the “stable” branch.

Two different Docker configurations are available that take on this task, with slightly different setups. Kyle Manna’s docker-openvpn is what I’m working with; it in turn is derived from jpetazzo/dockvpn which solves the same problem slightly differently.

There’s a tutorial on Digital Ocean that describes the setup including client-side support.

A second tutorial by Stefan Scherer gives instructions for use in the Scaleway ARM-based server cloud.

I’m at the stage right now where I downloaded and installed everything and created an “opvn” config file that successfully loads into Tunnelblick, but where it doesn’t quite yet have all of the routing set up. The slight complication in the configuration is that I want to give access to Amazon VPCs, so I have a few layers of software-defined networking to knit together (or to unravel depending on your perspective).

19 November 2015

Enjoy the attic while you can

Our attic is a three season room at best. In the depths of winter it’s really cold, and in the summer time it can be very hot. There’s a nod towards climate control but it’s a long way from the furnace and the space is just not really practical to equip as full fledged living space.

I have a work table up there (where I am now) with some tools, a few working shortwave radios, a couple of mostly working computers, and a fair measure of junk. It’s been the place where toys go when the boys have no interest in playing with them again, which is to say that everything that’s not Lego is up here.

As long as the weather stays mild, I have ambition to put the space to better use. At the bare minimum I’d like to get to the point where every day I get upstairs to do something, and where every time I come back down I have something in my hands to put to better use somewhere else or to throw away or recycle. I’ve tried to do marathon sorting and cleaning sessions and it’s just not me, but the every day approach at least gives you a chance for a series of small wins.

So, bit by bit, work at it. At some point it will get too cold, and let yourself quit then. Try not to do everything at once.

19 November 2015

Write one paragraph, floss one tooth

The a2b3 lunch today was at Madras Masala, we had a crowd of 10. I had a question about productivity - “what do you do every day” - and the answers were a good mix of attempts to be diligent and questions about whether narrowly focused productivity was even a good goal.

The small productivity approach that has stuck with me is BJ Fogg’s “Tiny Habits” approach, which he writes about on http://tinyhabits.com/ . The focus is not on New Years Resolution style “be a better person” goals, but on very small and attainable accompishments which you can use to build upon. “Floss one tooth” is the smallest example goal I can give, with the understanding that if you can build up a solid habit to do that every day then there’s lots more you can do for better dental health.

The writing equivalent of that is “write one paragraph”. I know that trying to blog every single day is a task I’ve tried and failed before, but I also know that it doesn’t take forever to write a paragraph, and you don’t have to have more than one idea to express to make that paragraph worthwhile. Faced with an impossibly big deadline for an impossibly big project, the thought goes, you might break down your resistance to working on the effort by declaring victory at the paragraph level.

Next week’s lunch (the day after Thanksgiving 2015) is at Morgan and York, hope to see you there.

19 November 2015

Alpine, old school email

alpine is an old school Unix mail user agent. It first arrived on the scene as pine in 1992, and when it appeared I remember how happy I was that there was finally a mailer that I could hand to people who had never seen Unix before and that they would get it and be productive on their 14.4 kb/sec dialup modems.

alpine appeared in 2007 as a rewrite of pine with Unicode support and a new Apache license. The version of it I’m running advertises itself as

Alpine 2.20 (OSX 67 2015-01-07) built Fri Oct 2 21:36:59 EDT 2015 on Edwards-MacBook-Air.local

and seems to work just fine. I’ve got it pointed at the old-school shared multiuser mail system that I’ve had for a long time at monkey.org and all is well.

The rubenerd weblog has a nice guide to installing alpine on OS X using your favorite package manager.

The biggest advantage of alpine over all web-based mail systems is just how fast you can delete mail. It’s easy to select by pattern, sort, refile and especially delete. In comparison, Google’s GMail feels clumsy and slow and impossibly mouse-dependent.

Have I moved all my email over to alpine? No. There’s a very small number of people who I prefer to send mail to that way, and it’s always good to have a fully functional backup email system that works perfectly well on a low-bandwidth setup. My workflow inside GMail is pretty fine tuned at this point and very different from how I used to use pine, and I’m not willing to go back.

It does remind me of the days before spam, and of the days when you were actually happy to get messages!

16 November 2015

Every day people

I admire people who can tackle an ambitious task every single day. Heck, I even admire them for doing some kind of nuisance task that still needs to be done every day.

My every day challenge is to write. It’s easy to get off that habit if you make too much of it, and it’s way too easy to endlessly read the endless Internet, mutter under your breath about how foolish it all is, and not write down your bit of foolishness in return.

So: write something, publish it, call it a success for today. The next day, reset the counters all to zero, and do the same. You don’t hope to have any dramatic results on day one, but maybe over time the results add up.

9 November 2015

When the FOIA request costs too much, ask for less

I asked for some documents from Washtenaw County, and they estimated that it would cost over $6100 to produce the records. Apparently the nature of the request was such that they would have had to review 1200 separate files each individually on paper to determine a response. I didn’t have a $6100 line item in my budget to pay for it, so the best charitable response is “no thanks”.

There is a question that I want to get at, though, and I think it’s attainable for less than $6100. How frequently does a restaurant owner appeal or protest the results of an inspection, and what sorts of things do they complain about? To get some idea of what’s going on I don’t need two years of inspection disputes; 30 days would do.

I don’t know how frequent these appeals are, whether there’s a form that gets filled out of if it’s done by phone or email, and what the results are. I do know that Washtenaw County routinely delays publication of routine inspections by 30 days or more to allow time for appeals.

Why the bother? Well, when a restaurant gets an awful inspection, it’s in the public interest to publicize that promptly rather than wait weeks for resolution. We saw this in Philadelphia, in this philly.com story

The city Health Department, which has had a long-standing policy of keeping restaurant inspection reports secret for 30 days, announced Monday that it will move to publicly post the reports “as quickly as possible.” …

The 30-day delay meant that diners could unknowingly patronize restaurants cited for serious hygiene problems.

I’m hopeful that 30 days worth of county records will be enough to discern a pattern. Do restaurant owners routinely challenge the results of inspections? Does the county modify their reports based on these challenges? Without knowing anything about how the process goes, the inclination I have is to want to have the same changes that were recently put in place in Philadelphia: prompt publication of serious hygeine problems is a benefit to public health.

Of these, the Downtown Area Citizens Advisory Council and the Downtown Development Authority post minutes to the DDA’s web site. The remainder have no obvious record or trail of their activities, decision making, or agendas.

The omission of minutes from two of these boards is particularly distressing. The Human Rights Commission labors in obscurity, not reporting out any of its meetings to the public, even as it is a focal point for the response to the shooting of Aura Rosser last year. And the Commission on Disability Issues doesn’t manage to produce a record that is accessible by people whose preferred medium is written.

I’ve been through the long, slow, drawn out ritual of asking the city for minutes of meetings before. In 2010, I sent in a series of FOIA requests that prompted the city’s Historic District Commission to produce meeting minutes from that year, after being originally told that those minutes did not exist. The city rectified that problem eventually, and now those minutes routinely show up on time.

Agendas of city meetings are key to understanding when you might want to lift yourself up from your easy chair and get downtown to weigh in on decision making at the city level. Minutes are important as a record of what actually happened. Groups like the Human Rights Commission, which is actively looking to advance its agenda, should be working harder to make sure that the people can see what they are up to. Otherwise you hold a meeting and spend time away from your kids and there’s no record of it - why did you bother?

Open Github issues and open source projects

As a first approximation, the number of open issues on an open source project that uses Github to track its issues is a metric of software complexity and project maturity.

It’s always worrisome to look at a project that has very few issues, because that’s usually a signal that no one else is using the code. Every bit of software has incomplete features that prompt a request, and bugs looking for a fix. No open issues at all suggest a dead project, or at least one that’s not actively attracting development.

Too many issues, on the other hand, suggest a system that’s so complex that no one person has a complete understanding of how the project works. The number of issues can grow beyond the point where a person could read and understand all the incoming bug reports and feature requests.

Every successful open source project deals with the struggle between adding new features to fill out the original vision of the designers and fixing enough bugs to make the system stable enough that it can be used in production without undue fears. This tug-of-war between features and bug fixes plays itself out over time, and sometimes difficult and complex bugs that only affect a few users get deprioritized over shiny new features that will attract a lot of new people to the project.

How many open issues can one person track? I’ll estimate that the number is about 100, plus or minus. So when a two person project has 50 open issues, it seems like it’s under control. A project that has over 1000 open issues (e.g. docker) can only raise some warning flags - how did it get to be so big? What sorts of issues can stick around for years and not get resolved? How on earth does one profess to have any overall understanding of a system that complex, short of devoting themselves to it?

1 November 2015

Open City Hall and government by survey

Ann Arbor’s “Open City Hall” is a survey instrument used to gather answers to multiple choice questions. Municipal officials - it’s not clear who - put together questions, and citizens are encouraged to tell the city what they think by providing answers. It’s run by Peak Democracy, which provides this same service to a number of municipalities.

The current question under review is development on the Library Lot in downtown Ann Arbor. The city has narrowed down the question to proposals from two developers, and then posts questions like these gems to try to gauge public sentiment:

CA Ventures' proposal has an attractive, aesthetically-pleasing
design and/or conveys an iconic image suitable for a site that is
in the core of downtown.

Core Ventures' proposal articulates a commitment to developing,
maintaining and managing an attractive public open-space.

Core Spaces' proposed development will provide a return on the
City’s investment in the Library Lot parking garage and contribute
to the tax base in Ann Arbor.

So far a grand total of 19 people have filled out this form, ticking one of the boxes (ranging from “strongly disagree” to “strongly agree”) giving their opinion. There’s no spot in the survey form for any kind of open-ended response about the proposals.

Does it make sense for a citizen to spend the time filling out these (poorly constructed) survey forms, or would it be better to spend your civic engagement time elsewhere? Checking check-boxes is about as weak a signal of engagement as I can imagine, and the low turnout means that the survey itself is not likely to be listened to very closely. You’d be better off writing a thoughtful letter and addressing it to someone - anyone, really - to share your opinion. Or show up at a public meeting (there are many of them) and say your 3 minutes when public comment time is there.

Checking check-boxes on survey forms is a far cry from citizen engagement.
