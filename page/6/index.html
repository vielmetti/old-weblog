Obituary: Dr. Lewis Ernest Peters (April 10, 1932 - March 4, 2015)

Dr. Lewis Ernest Peters, 82, of Marquette, died peacefully Wednesday morning, March 4, 2015, at Brookridge Heights Memory Care while under the care of Lake Superior Hospice.

He was born April 10, 1932, in Evanston, Illinois, a son of the late Lewis Ernest and Evelyn (Tehle) Peters. Lew married Katherine Kay Vielmetti in 1980 at the Marygrove Retreat Center in Garden, Michigan. He joined the faculty of the Biology Department at Northern Michigan University in 1961. During his tenure, Lew served as department head from 1971 to 1975, taught Anatomy, Physiology and Parasitology, and in 1980 received a contract from the National Park Service to study parasites at Isle Royale. He retired in 1996 after 35 years.

Lew was a member of St. Michael Catholic Church and Marquette Rotary Club. He is survived by his wife, Kathy Peters of Marquette; children, Raymond (Velquis) Peters of Indianapolis, IN, Matthew (Elizabeth) Peters of Austin, TX, Dan (Jennifer) Peters of Tallahassee, FL, Edward (Deborah Fisch) Vielmetti of Ann Arbor, MI, Stephen (Paula) Vielmetti of Milwaukee, WI and Anne (Douglas) Moody of Vancouver, WA; grandchildren, Nicolette & Elizabeth Peters, Zack and Ava Peters, Garrett, Bryson & Reese Peters, Saul and Jonathan Vielmetti, Marshall, Nathan, Sophia and Zevon Vielmetti, Graham and Spencer Moody; sister, Robyn (David) Messner of Jensen Beach, FL; numerous nieces & nephews; and special family friend's, Tim Glover and his family. He was preceded in death by his parents, first wife, Oneita "Beth" Peters and a brother, Edward Peters.

Lew's family would like to thank the staff of Brookridge Heights and Lake Superior Hospice for the wonderful care they provided.

A memorial service will be held on Monday, March 9, 2015 at 3:00 p.m. at the Canale-Tonella Funeral Home with Deacon David Adler officiating. Visitation will be held from 1:00 p.m. until the time of the service in the funeral home. Inurnment will take place at Park Cemetery. In lieu of flowers, memorials can be directed to the Biology Department at NMU in care of the NMU Foundation or the Lake Superior Land Conservancy.

The Canale-Tonella Funeral Home is assisting the family where memories may be shared at canalefuneral.com

Cleaning polygons in R and Shapely

The project of the week has been an effort to generate a set of maps - 2935 of them, in fact. The source data is from the Department of Energy, listing a set of utility companies and the counties they cover; the goal is to have a boundary map generated for each utility company. I know it won’t be a perfect boundary (most utility companies have at least one county that they only provide service to part of), but as a start it’s a pretty good one.

The DOE data is based on their Form EIA-861, and I’m working from the 2013 data set. See Electric power sales, revenue, and energy efficiency Form EIA-861 detailed data files if you want to get your own files. It’s provided as Excel (XLSX) files, so you’ll need something compatible to read them; I ended up using in2csv from csvkit as the first part of the pipeline to break these apart.

You get lines from the provided file that look like this:

Data Year,Utility Number,Utility Name,State,County
2013,5109,DTE Electric Company,MI,Shiawassee
2013,5109,DTE Electric Company,MI,St Clair
2013,5109,DTE Electric Company,MI,Tuscola
2013,5109,DTE Electric Company,MI,Washtenaw
2013,5109,DTE Electric Company,MI,Wayne

The next challenge is to pull a map for each county served. To do this, convert the (State,County) list to a FIPS code, using a table like this one from ORNL called CoFIPS00.txt. (MI,Washtenaw) turns into 26161. Feed a list of FIPS codes into the Census Reporter geo API, and out comes GeoJSON files, like so: GeoJSON for Marquette and Alger Counties, Michigan.

We have a list of counties for each utility, so at minimum we're set right there. But we need to get 2935 of them, and it's kind of slow to wait for each one to return before fetching the next one. That motivates the use of GNU Parallel, a shell tool for executing jobs in parallel. On my four core MacBook Air I ran 32 jobs in parallel, and 2935 fetches (using "curl") took 287 seconds with the median elapsed time for each job at about 3 seconds - about a 31x speedup vs doing things serially.

However, the GeoJSON provided is really one shape per county, and we're looking for a complete service area in a single shape, so we need to merge the result together. Here a useful tool is the "R" stats package, particularly the "rgeos", "rgdal", and "maptools" packages.

The first step is the readOGR() function from the rgdal library. It takes a variety of input map formats including GeoJSON and shape files and pulls them into a Spatial vector object in R. rgdal is an R interface to Frank Warmerdam's Geospatial Data Abstraction Library, and it handles both raster and vector geospatial data formats.

Next, use the unionSpatialPolygons() function from the maptools library to merge the county polygons into a single polygon. The docs say that this is a wrapper around the gUnion() function from the rgeos library. rgeos comes from the Google Summer of Code 2010, and this introduction to the project explains the motivation. rgeos provides an R interface to GEOS, which is in turn a C++ port of JTS, the Java Topology Suite.

After you run the unionSpatialPolygons() operation, you will inevitably find that there are tiny holes in your unioned map. Geographic boundaries are complex, and as a result there are often little areas that don't quite touch, leaving little bits of land that aren't included anywhere. This algorithm from stackoverflow, Dissolve holes in polygon in R, takes care of the issue. 

G.rings = Filter(function(f){f@ringDir==1},G.union@polygons[[1]]@Polygons)
G.bounds = SpatialPolygons(list(Polygons(G.rings,ID=1)))

As the Polygon documentation note for ringDir: "the ring direction of the ring (polygon) coordinates, holes are expected to be anti-clockwise"

Having done this cleanup work, we return a GeoJSON file with the toGeoJSON() function from the leafletR package. Now I probably don't need to pull in all of leafletR; there's a writeOGR() function in the rgdal package that should also do the same work. The aside, though, is that leafletR creates nice maps in R using the Leaflet Javascript library.

With all that done, we have a new GeoJSON file that's a cleaned up merge of multiple county outlines done in R. However, the process isn't quite done. Some of the merged files don't have holes, but they do have "slivers", little angular bits of geometry from where two counties don't quite touch at their corners. I didn't find an algorithm to unsliver in R, but this Removing small polygons gaps in a Shapely polygon did the trick. 

# Here's the algorithm
fx = poly.buffer(eps, 1, join_style=JOIN_STYLE.mitre).buffer(-eps, 1, join_style=JOIN_STYLE.mitre)

Shapely from Sean Gilles is a "Python package for set-theoretic analysis and manipulation of planar features using (via Python’s ctypes module) functions from the well known and widely deployed GEOS library." So we're back to GEOS, which is also supported by R...which means I suspect this algorithm translates directly back into R by proper invocation of the equivalent gBuffer() function from the rgeos package.

No matter, I installed Shapely. It's pretty fast, and if I had time to refine this operation I'd try to port all of the R code back into python using Shapely to see if I could get a meaningful speedup improvement.

15 February 2015

Designing a wearable magnetic sensor

I woke up this morning thinking about ways to improve my sense of direction. (Don't ask me why.) Here's some previous work on the internet that I had found for wearable devices that give you an always-on compass reading and some ideas for more.

First, some prior art. How to make a tactile compass feedback belt is a typical design, with about $150 in parts. In short you wire up an electronic compass to a series of tiny cell-phone style vibrator motors, and you position a dozen or so motors along a belt. When you're pointing in a certain direction, the belt vibrates in that spot, and you get a continuous sense of direction. Wired Magazine wrote this up in 2007, and the invention of the "feelSpace belt" is credited to Osnabrück cognitive scientist Peter König. Here's a CBC interview with Peter König from 2014.

If you look at the designs for these magnetic belts, you quickly discover that the electronics to detect direction are relatively straightforward, and that there are lots of wires and buzzers that you have to harness together. So the question I have is whether there's a better way to encode direction that doesn't require a dozen motors! You'd think that you could use a code on a single vibrotactile device to "tap out" a direction, or use a pair of devices to encode "northiness" and "eastiness" to give the whole code with two points instead of a dozen. There would be a training time to learn this, and it might be less obvious than a single motor pointing north. The evident advantage of the two-motor approach over the twelve-motor approach is that the wearable device could be very small.

Just how cold is that window? Winter monitoring with the TI Sensortag, Node-RED, and Freeboard

Our living room has lovely (old) wooden windows. When it's cold, the windows get cold. Last year we got some lovely thermal curtains to insulate.

I thought I'd use some of the equipment I had to see just how cold the windows were and how much warmer the curtains are to the touch.

The setup:

Macbook Air running OS X 10.9 (Mavericks), Node-RED 0.9.1, Freeboard, TI Sensortag. I'm using RabbitMQ as my MQTT message passing system, but that's not strictly necessary for this application.

The Macbook has support for Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) which is what the Sensortag talks. Support for BLE in Node-RED is provided by the "noble" module, which the "sensortag" module uses to communicate with the device. Freeboard is a visualization dashboard with some simple widgets to display data coming to it.

This report on how to use the Freeboard dashboard with Node-RED has all of the difficult parts. It provides a websocket listener for Freeboard, and you use the built-in websocket transmitter on Node-RED. There's a very tiny bit of logic to pull apart the data coming off the Sensortag; it could probably be less if I were more clever.

and the very simple dashboard I put together to test this looks like this


There's a catch to using the TI Sensortag; it sometimes turns itself off, to save battery. To correct this you can install new firmware on the device to disable the battery saving mode. The folks at MyWeatherCenter provide a link to their firmware which deals with the issue, at the cost of lower battery life.

The results: the windows are way, way colder than the inside air (about 45 degrees), but the curtains read much warmer (about 60 degrees). Thermal curtains for the win!

12 January 2015

Vellum, a downtown Ann Arbor restaurant, has closed

Vellum closed in January 2015. The Ann Arbor News story tells the part of the story of an innovative kitchen that was "like a laboratory", and a subsequent format change to a more mainstream menu. The restaurant had a two year run.

What's missing from the narrative is the prompt from the county health department that forced the change. Vellum's menu at the outset used the "sous vide" cooking technique to produce its most innovative dishes. County health officials took a dim view of this, as seen in this excerpt from a health inspection in February 2014:

(Critical) Found a package of meat in a vacuum sealed package, and found beef short rib in a vacuum sealed package in the basement meat walk in cooler. These packages were the same size as the vacuum package supply in facility, and did not appear to be commercial products as there were no labels and pic could not provide any statements or boxes indicating from a commercial licensed source. Pic corrected by discarding meats in vacuum sealed packages. Found a piece of meat in a vacuum sealed package with " 140 degrees 4 and half hours" written on package in the walk in cooler. Pic states he does not know what marking mean and where this came from. Pic corrected by discarding meat. Found several pieces of sous vide equipment in the basement and main kitchen areas. Found at least 5 boxes of packages for vacuum packaging on the shelf in the basement prep area, and was denied access to room/closet next to shelf with vacuum packaging. To correct verify that no one in facility is using vacuum sealer or preparing food using sous vide method as a variance is required. This violation has repeated and a risk control plan is required to be submitted.

If your innovative kitchen depends on methods that are in violation of regulations, it's going to be hard to keep up the buzz when you have to give them up.

3 January 2015

Olivetti Programma 101

The first computer that I ever had all to myself was the Olivetti Programma 101. This was a 1965-era Italian machine that was just a little more than a programmable calculator. It used flexible magnetic cards for program storage, discrete logic for all of its computing functions, delay line memory (240 bytes), and it all sat in a 65 pound package. Output was on a humming little printer built into the machine.

Graveraet Middle School in Marquette had one of these, presumably discarded from its use in the school administration. By 1977 the machine was so completely obsolete that it sat idle, with no one knowing how it worked. I got plenty of time to spend with the system, and frankly I never really figured it out either. It was easy enough to use it as an adding and multiplying and dividing machine, but the mysteries of its program logic escaped me.

There are several folks who have active projects to restore the old system, including a project at the DataMuseum in Denmark. See their efforts here. There are quite a lot of mechanical parts in this old machine, including a set of 8 pulleys that drove the various bits of the card reader and the printer. Rubber rollers of course get old and decayed with time so every account of a restoration has some discussion of that repair.

The photo at the top is from Michigan Tech, written by James D. Spain, who outlines the use of the Programma 101 in the biology program at Tech in 1969.

Here is a video, “Quando Olivetti inventò il PC”, with a history showing the machine in action.

What we don't know about AirAsia Flight 8501

Twitter and Facebook are good at moving information quickly around the globe, but they send sense and nonsense in equal parts, and it's easy to read things that are speculation or even outright falsehoods.

Wikipedia's page on Indonesia AirAsia Flight 8501 warns "Initial news reports may be unreliable". At the moment the page is up to date with as much of the fragments of news that have been made available, and it links to "unconfirmed and speculative early reports" that speak of wreckage being found.

Begger's funnel plot: a marker for irregularities in scientific publication

From Scientific American, [For Sale: “Your Name Here” in a Prestigious Science Journal] (http://www.scientificamerican.com/article/for-sale-your-name-here-in-a-prestigious-science-journal/)

“Beggers funnel plot” is particularly revealing. There is no such thing as a Beggers funnel plot. "It doesn't exist. That's the point," says Guillaume Filion, a biologist at the Center for Genomic Regulation in Barcelona, Spain (pdf). A statistician named Colin Begg and another statistician named Matthias Egger each invented tests and tools to look for biases that creep into meta-analyses. "Begger's funnel plot" appears to be an accidental hybrid of the two names.

The problem? A steep rise in the number of "meta-analysis" papers published in China, many of which seem to be following a common template for reporting results - including sharing typographic errors, mis-attribution, and formulaic text.

The best ongoing reporting on dishonesty in the scientific literature is Retraction Watch from Ivan Oransky and Adam Marcus. Filion's post on A flurry of copycats on PubMed digs deeper into the problem, studying the copycat formula:

The real shock came when we downloaded the papers. Most are behind paywalls, so we could access only 25 of them. There was no need for more, the pattern was very clear from the third study. The papers have exactly the same structure, with the same figures in the same order. The last figure of each paper is a “Begger’s funnel plot”, featuring a surrealistic fusion of Begg and Egger (Colin Begg and Matthias Egger both gave their name to a test for publication bias). What struck me the most was the similarity in the aesthetics of the figures, the same line widths, the same colors, the same shapes etc.

Listening to Radio Havana Cuba via shortwave

Radio Havana Cuba was one of my cold war era shortwave radio presets. I don't have a working shortwave radio of my own any more, and the miracle of the Internet means that you can now tune in broadcasts directly from the broadcaster without the inconvenience of shortwave tuning.

The best way really to listen to a station like R Havana Cuba is via shortwave. Here are a couple of ways that you can do that. Note that broadcasts in English are not 24 hours a day, and that shortwave reception varies widely by time of day and location of receiving station.

The key to do this is to acquaint yourself with WebSDR, a project that allows multiple listeners to tune into the signal of a single wideband radio. You'll need to pick a receiver that has an antenna that covers the appropriate part of the broadcast band, and you'll need also a schedule for when the station is broadcasting.

Many shortwave schedules and stations can be located via short-wave.info.


Given a schedule and a frequency, use WebSDR to find a suitable listening location. My two favorites for monitoring Havana are the original WedSDR at U Twente and K2SDR in New Jersey.

Of course you can tune in via TuneIn and get to the broadcast direct. The sound will be more clear, and the reception more reasonable. But there's really something to be said for experiencing broadcast radio through the medium of radio rather than this newfangled Internet thing.

Some notes on Echolink and websdr

Echolink is a system that lets amateur radio operators transmit from their computer, through a relay at a suitable Echolink server. You have to be a licensed ham to get on the network, and typically there's a two-way repeater set up to some radio station so that you can hear what's being said on RF and vice versa.

websdr is a system that lets anyone listen to a slice of radio spectrum. There are websdr servers running around the globe, each tuned in to some particular slice of the radio waves. Multiple people can tune in at the same time and hear their own piece of the airwaves.

The interesting thing happens when you connect these two systems together. Some small number of the websdr receivers tune into the 2 meter band, which is the typical band used by Echolink. Using Echolink (or in my case the Mac application Echomac) I identified an Echolink host in the vicinity of the websdr receiver, then connected to it. Sure enough, I was able to hear my call sign back in through the websdr front end.

This little image shows the result. Transmitted via DM0ZHS, and monitored at http://129.187.5.188:8901/ on 145237.50 kHz, I'm sending from Isen, Germany and receiving the transmission at the Olympic Tower in Munich, Germany.

Obituary: John Gall, 1925-2014

Gall, John C. 9/18/1925 - 12/15/2014 Walker, MN John C. Gall, Jr., beloved husband, father, pediatrician, scholar and author, passed away with his wife by his side Dec. 15, 2014, in Walker, MN, from natural causes. He was 89. John was born on September 18, 1925 in Washington, D.C. and grew up near Upperville, VA. In 1945 he was drafted into the Army Air Corps and sent to Germany, where he served as a medic in the Allied army of occupation. During his tour of duty he attended the Nuremberg trials, which kindled in him a lifelong passion to heal the afflicted and promote justice. Upon returning home he furthered his education, graduating from Yale and George Washington University Medical School. He was a postdoctorate fellow at the Mayo Clinic, where he was dubbed “the best pediatric diagnostician [they had] ever seen!” In addition he earned a certification in Human Genetics and completed 7 years of therapy training. Dr. Gall began his medical career as a researcher at the University of Virginia, followed by a 3-year stint at the Geisinger Clinic in Danville, PA. He next went into private practice in Miles City, MT, but was soon invited to teach at the University of Michigan Medical School in Ann Arbor, MI. There he also served as head of the Plymouth State Home, where he did research partially funded by the Kennedy family foundation, and named and described several syndromes of the developmentally disabled . In addition he established a long-running private pediatric practice of 38 years. During this period he developed a special appreciation for, and an enduring interest in, the importance of nonverbal communication between very young children and their parents. This led him to expand his practice to include support and education classes, in which he was aided by his late wife, Beth, and then by his wife Carol. Their observations and anecdotes are included in Elegant Parenting, written with wife, Beth; in Dancing with Elves written by John, and with wife Carol he wrote Hit by a Low-Flying Goose. John’s best known book is System Antics, later published under the title The Systems Bible, a humorous but insightful study of the problems and pitfalls that afflict every type of system that human beings create-whether mechanical, economic, corporate, aeronautical, legal, linguistic, religious, medical or electronic. He was very proud that the great Swiss psychiatrist, Alice Miller, who introduced the world to the subject of child sexual abuse, endorsed his work before she died. He also spoke at a symposium at Princeton University entitled “Children under the Shadow of War.” The group included scholars from around the world, and filmmakers who had photographed kidnapped children who had been conscripted into war and forced to commit atrocities. Establishing a routine for processing inbound communications

I used Google Inbox for a little while, but gave up on it. It turns out that the routine I have with Google's Gmail for handling inbound correspondence is pretty good, and Inbox was too different from Gmail to make it worthwhile to do an easy transition.

I don't know if my workflow for handling message traffic is perfect, but it seems to be pretty good. Email comes in, I read it, and anything that deserves further attention but can't be handled right away is starred. The next round of handling works its way through the starred messages, and when something in that queue is stuck and needs more attention than I can get to at the moment it goes into a Github issue. Periodically I work through Github, sort out some priority items, and try to crank through the queues.

The problem with systems is that they periodically fail. My system works OK as well as it goes, but it can fall down when I get a whole series of messages from one person right one after the other. I'm accustomed to a routine where in most cases there's one or two things outstanding that need to be resolved on any ongoing conversation, and the burst of unanswered email gets in the way of that effort.

If you have a system that allows you to accumulate a queue, there will inevitably be delays. I like being absolutely on top of my inbox and able to answer queries as they come in, and there's some basic capacity in messages per hour that I can process when I'm "on". Even emptying out a queue into an issue tracker can take time, and thus inevitably there's some things that don't get done instantly.

In periodic posts to Twitter I try to track my backlog. In truth there's always something that will cause an event in my todo list to be overtaken by events, at a pace of about one or two a day. The key mostly for me is just to keep things flowing through, and to try very hard not to get so bogged down in the moment that I can't tackle tougher tasks that actually need more than 10 minutes of concentration.

Github-based kanban boards: Zenhub, Waffle, Huboard, Blossom et al

A kanban board is simply a two-dimensional view of a series of work queues. By moving a piece of work from one column to another, you update its status. This gives you a quick way to visualize your current priorities, as well as making it very clear when one or more of your queues are too full.

I've been using Github issues as a personal issue tracker for a while, using its milestones feature to track categories. This has been mostly a one-dimensional exercise of managing very long lists, or shorter lists. It's been cumbersome to use either labels or milestones to handle the quick need to say to yourself "I'm going to focus on this now" without the issue sinking back quickly into a mile-long undifferentiated list.

Enter a whole series of Github add-ons that all add a similar feature, the kanban-style column view. I'll list them here with any experience I currently have using them. My best bet is that at some point Github absorbs one of these and makes it its own, but that is tempered by the realization that Github issues really isn't changing much as a product.

Zenhub, zenhub.io. This is a Google Chrome plugin, which gives you a kanban-style view of your issues. It only works in Chrome. I tried it, but didn't see an easy way to configure which columns referred to which labels, and after a few tries gave up on it.

Waffle, waffle.io. At the moment this is what I'm using. You authenticate to it with Github, and then it displays your issues. Free (for now), they are advertising that it will be $7/mo for private repos, which is what I currently pay for all of the rest of Github.

Huboard, huboard.com. Free for open source repos or $7/mo/user for private repos. 14 day free trial.

Blossom, blossom.io. No free plan, 14 day trial, minimum is a 5 user plan at $22/mo.

Task Tub, tasktub.com. Company page does not load.

SweepBoard, sweepboard.com. Free for open source projects.

When I first started finding these sorts of things, I didn't really realize that it was a whole category of products. Most of them have some other features beyond the kanban to add to Github as a project management tool. I don't think there's a category winner, but if you are interested in visualizing your work and taking your todo list beyond the simple long list this is a useful set of tools to explore.
