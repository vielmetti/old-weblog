29 October 2015

Diligence

There are a lot of little boxes to type into on the Internet. Twitter and Facebook will gladly accept your text and send it out into the world. Medium is there if you want to be high minded, and Slack will let you narrow your message to a choice few. And there’s always Github issues, any of thousands of projects will accept a comment on an issue, real or imagined.

The burden of diligence of blogging is to stick to a single system over a long time, to drag around your past with you and keep it online, and to feed the system frequently enough that it (and you) are not forgotten.

The diligent blogger would not only have something to say every day but they’d say it in the same place and connect it somehow with all other things they had said on the same topic. Can you blame people at all for picking up other new systems and trying them out? The blank canvas can be a lot more appealing than the infinite scroll when you want to try something new.

21 October 2015

Over the top networks

I’m giving a talk on Over The Top Networks at the Radical/Networks conference in Brooklyn this weekend, October 24-25 2015.

I don’t know exactly what I’m going to say in what order yet, but… the best way to predict that is to write down some notes. So here we go.

The premise, briefly stated, is that most successful networks run “over the top” of existing working networks, and that you can learn a lot from what goes wrong along the way when the underlying network fights back.

Lots of examples, here’s just some of them.

–

APRS is a worldwide location sharing system that runs over amateur packet radio. It runs “over the top” of VHF radios, and sends out location information for participating nodes to digital repeaters (“digipeaters”) that relay the signal to others in the participating mesh. It’s designed initially to be completely free of external infrastructure dependencies, except for some good source of location information provided by GPS.

This network worked fine at first, but as it got more popular, the network got more congested. A single transmission from a moving vehicle would trigger two or three rounds of repeaters resending the message, and in places where the network was dense with stations there would be collisions on the single radio channel. To compensate, some stations would set their initial broadcast to repeat four or five times, increasing their own chance of getting heard at the expense of more congestion network-wide.

The solution ended up being fairly simple, once the Internet got to be reliable enough. An internet gateway (“I-Gate”) was set up to capture location information shared by radios, so that instead of transmitting this data via collision-prone VHF radios, it went over the Internet. A much small number of internet-enabled gateways were able to handle the load, and it allowed stations that only had Internet connections to participate in the network by sending their location reports directly to the I-Gate.

There are all sorts of radio lessons to be learned from the saga of APRS. Stations sharing a single low-speed narrow band radio channel have to work extra hard not to collide with each other, and the “hidden terminal” problem (where a station interferes with another station that it can’t hear, but that can hear it) ensures that network density leads to more problems.

–

Usenet News started in 1979 in North Carolina, connecting computers in the Research Triangle between Duke and the University of North Carolina. It grew rapidly in the 1980s, taking advantage of whatever networks were available to it. The relatively simple Unix-to-Unix Copy approach to networking meant that virtually any data network that could be coaxed into sending a data stream unmodified over a circuit could be put to use.

Usenet ran over the top of dialup phone lines in its early times, and this posed a problem: long distance calls cost money. Several methods were used to great effect to get away from this financial limitation on network growth.

One very simple way to bypass telephone bills is to have the telephone company call you. Researchers at Bell Labs, the home of Unix, had a great interest in seeing the network succeed, and thus they routinely set up their systems to make large numbers of outbound telephone calls to poll remote stations for mail and news. The costs of toll long distance telephone service were hidden inside the phone company’s own phone bills, as well as the phone bills of several other large computer companies where agile systems staff were able to avoid the attention of bean counters (who would probably not have approved of spending corporate dollars to retransmit rec.pets.cats).

A parallel approach was to reuse dialup networks originally designed for terminals to connect to mainframe computers and repurpose them for machine-to-machine use. In Michigan, the statewide MERIT computer network was designed to connect research and education computing across the state. Clever systems programmers (including yours truly) realized that it could be also used to support slow but functional UUCP connections, and that those links would not incur any per-hour or per-kilobyte charges. Thus a bulletin board system in Michigan’s remote Upper Peninsula could get free worldwide email and Usenet news feeds by befriending the right people in Ann Arbor and adapting the particular arcane configuration.

As Usenet grew, the demand for bandwidth started to outstrip the capacity of dialup telephone circuits. This led to efforts to use and reuse the new source of “free” long-distance data services, namely the evolving Internet of its day. By the time the National Science Foundation had christened its super-fast, 1.5 megabit per second network, Usenet News admins were ready with the NNTP protocol which could take best advantage of the new network.

It’s estimated that over the 10 year lifespan of the NSFnet that public sources contributed over $2 billion to the network - $200 million direct from the NSF, and the rest from a variety of state sources. By 1995, Usenet News was consuming about 8% of the bandwidth provided by the network. If that’s a plausible overall measure of the relative cost of that traffic, that means that Usenet News could have had as much as a $160 million subsidy over 10 years to grow and expand on the public dime. A good system to build on top of!

One of the challenges of building a successful network is that at some point you look like a system that can be exploited for other people’s use, just in the same way that your system exploited some aspect of the surrounding environment to get its toehold into the world. Two moments in the early 1990s development of Usenet show signs of what would be its demise as a discussion forum dominiated by researchers and graduate students.

Up until September 1993, the ebb and flow of Usenet as a discussion medium was pretty predictable. A network dominated by graduate students, Usenet typically got a lot of new users in September, who floundered around until they either dropped off the net or sorted out how to use it effectively. A year later another September would roll around, and last year’s new users were now this year’s seasoned veterans, showing the newbies around or harrassing them or both.

September 1993 changed all of that because America Online, the biggest online service at the time, decided to add Usenet access to its millions of commercial customers. Not surprisingly, these new users were different from the ones that came before them - they were more often that not far removed from the academic and research and hobbyist networks that had come before them on Usenet. A culture clash ensued, and in some ways Usenet never recovered.

AOL’s embrace of Usenet is a classic “over the top” maneuver. AOL did not have to hire any new moderation staff, run transcontinental networks, or invent any new protocols to join in this existing network. Instead, they could gather all of the benefits of providing their user base with access to new services at a very small marginal cost.

Taking advantage of Usenet is also the theme of advertising networks that took heed of Usenet’s worldwide low cost distribution system to plant their own unsolicited commercial messages.

Usenet had always been somewhat commercialized, ever since the first person posted an ad for a dinette set for sale in New Jersey. Employment advertising was tolerated or even welcomed, and regional “forsale” groups prefigured Craigslist in many ways. But there was a social contract that kept cooperating systems away from wholesale advertising. Sending articles around the world costs money, and if the powers that be knew that you were sending ads around on their dime they’d kick you (or us) off the net.

By 1994, though, the social contract had weakened a bit. Commericial internet service providers were selling dialup Usenet accounts, and AOL was offering rec.pets.cats to the masses. How natural would it be for someone to observe that they could get huge numbers of ad impressions for little or no money to a receptive audience?

Enter the “Green Card Lawyers”, Canter and Siegel. With the help of a programmer they designed an ad campaign that would promote their immigrant legal aid to over 5500 separate newsgroups at once. This burst of traffic - a separate ad for each and every Usenet newsgroup they could find - was so substantial that they managed to take some parts of the network offline (New Zealand, in particular, which had some of the highest costs of importing Usenet anywhere, disconnected temporarily rather than transmit the onslaught).

Sadly, the Usenet community response to the Green Card Lawyers and their ilk was a sign that the community infrastructure of Usenet was going to have to adapt to a world where some members of the network were hostile and were going to try to subvert the cooperative enterprise with advertising. Unsolicited ads all sorts of things followed - sketchy pharmaceutical advertisements promoting male enhancement drugs were perhaps the most typical. The Usenet architecture started to crumble, as people looking to escape the noise fled to private email lists, and as institutions supporting the net looked twice at their costs and decided it might not be worth it.

I keep coming back to Usenet because it’s so fascinating, and I spent so much time there, and surprisingly enough it’s still going. What can over the top networks learn from its surprising longevity, even though several generations of technologists have given up on it?

Usenet is the prototypical mesh network. Individual nodes in the network connect to each other, without needing any central authority to bless them. They connect using whatever technology they agree to, without needing to adhere to any central standard except for the format of news articles. The network works with a “flooding” protocol that distributes texts to all parts of the network that are connected that want to receive them, and aspects of the flooding algorithm ensure efficiency and that no one gets an article sent to them twice. For a 1979 design, it’s held up well.

What has changed irrevocably from the original is the content on the network. Once upon a time, in the days before widespread use of the Internet, it served as an amalgam of what emerged as all sorts of independent commercial services. Modern day Github is an echo of the old comp.sources.* hierarchy, delivering source code and patches and enhancements to your door. Quora and Stack Overlow try to replicated the comp.* newsgroups, and Reddit handles most of rec.*. Many if not most of the original newsgroups are dead, overrun by spam.

What lives on is the alt.* newsgroups, in particular alt.binaries.*. These were never welcomed by the old guard who remembered how expensive it was to transmit anything over a modem, but now over 30 years later the cost of the network is so low.

The modern Usenet ends up being a vast expanse of digitized video, and a host of specialized newsreaders designed to quickly scoop down the movie or TV show that you want to watch. The distribution and flooding algorithms are perfect for this task, and copyright owners have to work extra hard because even taking down one copyrighted work from one part of the network doesn’t prevent you from pulling it from some other area.

I want to fast forward a bit from the 1990s to the modern day, to illustrate that all sorts of contemporary networks have bootstrapped their way to success on top of other, older infrastructure.

The first common pattern I’ll call “identity proxy”. The idea here is that you can take advantage of an existing organization’s identification process, without ever having to know someone’s login and password. By extending privileges to anyone who can prove that they are a member of some other organization, you can scoop up valuable early users.

Facebook is one excellent example of this, especialy in its early days. How do you restrict access to your brand new social network so that only the social elites can participate? Simple, the onboarding process for your new network runs over the top of the email system of the network you want to attract from. Anyone who wants an account has to prove that they can get an email from a harvard.edu address. Voila, you have just run your login system over the top of a very expensive and valuable college admissions process, at minimal cost to yourself.

Slack runs the same strategy when trying to recruit companies to use its product. You can configure a Slack instance so that anyone who has an email address in a particular domain is automatically approved. This gives Slack leverage to gather employees for its “free” service and insight to get those people into its paid service.

“Identity proxy” is a powerful pattern. If you find the right trove of people who have a desirable email address, you can scoop them up into your system and exclude others who don’t have the right qualifying talisman.

The state of the city, Ann Arbor, fall 2015

A perspective on Ann Arbor area local government, as seen from fall 2015.

The top level view is that local units of government are struggling with their technology investments, having a difficult time keeping them working, and are unwilling or unable to commit to any sort of “open data” initiatives as a result of their difficulties with basic operations.

Some examples will illustrate.

The City of Ann Arbor keeps track of a wide range of planning and permit documents through a vendor system called eTrakit. This system was down for more than a week in September, and still occasionally crashes in response to a query. There is no general purpose “open data” query to get access to the system, though the city does publish a “permits under review” feed on its open data catalog that has a small subset of the available data. Attachments in the system were recently taken offline after unredacted credit card numbers were discovered.

City of Ann Arbor meeting agendas and minutes and documents are locked up in a vendor-provided Legistar system, which is so difficult to use that many boards and commissions don’t even bother to publish their minutes through that system. Meeting agendas are often delayed in publication, and there is no method available to search across the whole system for documents.

The Ann Arbor City Clerk keeps track of FOIA requests across the city and publishes a “database” of requests. As is all too typical for municipal government, this “database” is published as a PDF file, not as plain text in some format that can be easily parsed.

The City Liquor License Review Committee routinely evaluates applications for new permits to serve or manufacture alcohol. These applications are not published as part of the agenda for that committee or for Ann Arbor City Council’s online system.

I would go on, but the City Council meeting is tonight, and I’m preparing something for public commentary (probably to add to the discussion only after the decisions have been made).

August 2015 food inspections for Washtenaw County

Every month the Washtenaw County Health Department releases reports from a previous month’s inspections of restaurants in the county. Restaurants are typically inspected twice a year. The inspector looks to see that the state Food Code is enforced, with a particular eye towards identifying violations that result in increased risk to the public of food-borne illness.

Wayne Eaker has put together a searchable and browsable database of these inspections, which is online at food-inspections.annarbortelegraph.com . Here are some highlights and lowlights from the August 2015 report.

19 restaurants and food service establishments had zero violations in August 2015. Among those perfect scores: Zingerman’s Next Door, Earthen Jar, BTB Cantina on South U, Little Porky’s in Whitmore Lake, World of Beers, Classic Pizza in Dexter, and Sweetwaters on Plymouth Road.

Yee Siang Dumplings at 4837 Washtenaw received a total of 17 violations. At the August 21, 2015 inspection the issues identifed included “unsafe cooling methods”, a dirty meat slicer and meat grinder, and no soap at the rear employee hand sink. “NO ONE IN THIS STORE HAS ANY KNOWLEDGE OF FOOD SAFETY AND HOW TO IMPLEMENT THE FOOD CODE IN THEIR OPERATION.”

Tony Sacco’s on W Eisenhower Pkwy had 19 violations in their August 5, 2015 inspection. “OBSERVED LIVE ANTS AND NUMEROUS SMALL FLIES IN THE AREAS SURROUNDING THE FLOOR DRAIN IN THE WAITSTATION.”

Grange Kitchen and Bar was dinged for “OBSERVED UNLABELED MASON JARS OF DUCK BLOOD IN CHEST FREEZER IN KITCHEN.” Their August 27, 2015 inspection recorded a total of 8 violations.

Aventura at 216 East Washington received a total of 7 violations in their August 18, 2015 inspection. “OBSERVED SEVERAL EMPLOYEES IN BAR AREA HANDLE RTE FOOD WITH BARE HANDS” was noted, as well as a practice of re-labeling datemarked foods to extend their shelf life. “EMPLOYEE STATED THAT IT IS COMMON PRACTICE FOR KITCHEN STAFF TO SMELL OR TASTE FOOD PRODUCTS WHEN THEY ARE ON THEIR LAST DAY OF SALE AND RE-DATE FOOD FOR ANOTHER 5 DAYS IF THEY THINK THE FOOD IS STILL GOOD. THIS PRACTICE MUST BE CEASED IMMEDIATELY.”

How to squash a commit in git

If you are working on a project in Github, your work isn’t always linear. Sometimes the change you make doesn’t work completely, so you back a little bit of it out and move forward on a next part. It may take some hacking and sawing (and testing of course) to get a change that just works right.

Projects like to have contributions, but project maintainers don’t like to insert noise into their efforts. The ideal commit to a project is completely clean: one single commit that incorporates all of the changes that you want to do, without any of the intervening cruft.

Thanks to Alan Gutierrez for the help here.

In the lingo, this is known as “squashing a commit”. It turns A->B->C->D->X->Y->Z into A->Z. Here’s how you do it.

First, set up your environment before you start to work. Create a branch to put your changes in and work within that branch. You can do all of that from within Github, or from the command line as

% git checkout -b try/branch
Switched to a new branch 'try/branch'

When you’re ready to merge, create a pull request for that branch. That will gather together all of the changes you have made and drop them onto a single page for inspection. Look at the first change that you made in that sequence, and find the parent node for your first change; we’ll rebase off of that parent. This was the crucial bit that I was unable to figure out from the documentation.

Squash all of those commits for the branch into a single commit. There is no git squash command, alas; but there is git rebase -i.

% git rebase -i <after-this-commit>

Next, apply this single squashed commit to your master branch.

Further reading:

  • From Git Ready: Squashing commits with rebase
  • Makandra Cards: Squash several Git commits into a single commit
  • David Walsh: Squash commits with Git
  • Pro Git, Scott Chacon and Ben Straub: Git Tools - Rewriting History

Snap CI

Snap CI is a cloud-based continuous integration system. Like Travis CI, which I have written about here, it lives in the cloud, lets you run programs triggered by GitHub commits, and offers a testing environment for free for open source projects which lets you familiarize yourself with the system before deciding to pay for it for your non-open-source efforts.

I’m looking at all of these tools now because this weblog is now managed through a process that involves checking things into Github, which makes it completely suitable for automation for common tasks every time I save a file. (Spell checking, link checking, really any kind of quality control you can imagine reducing to a tiny shell script.)

Like Travis CI, Snap CI lets you automate the build process. Where it diverges (and where it’s interesting) is that the build process is only the first part of a multi-stage pipeline that includes deployment at the end of the task only when a set of human decisions have been made as to whether the thing you made is ready to be shared.

This Snap CI blog post on why Travis CI and Snap CI are not the same thing is from Snap CI and it’s a useful overview.

The downside of multi-stage pipelines for deployment is that they are very hard to automate correctly, and very hard to demonstrate correct behavior with open source projects that generally struggle to have any automated test facilities at all. So while Travis CI is a very good fit for open source components (and has attracted a whole sub-industry of writing .travis.yml files to support those), it really doesn’t have the whole “continuous deployment” pattern sorted out. And while Snap CI looks like it would be great for saying “once we’re happy with this, push it out to the world”, that setup is not easy to pick up simply by inspecting other projects that have done it.

Into the matrix with Travis CI

Travis CI is a continuous integration tool, a description which means nothing to people who have never used it. “Continuous integration” is one of these industry phrases that doesn’t serve to illuminate the problem that it describes.

Why would you care? Well, you’re working on a project with a bunch of other people, and it’s kind of complicated. It might depend on some old code that’s fragile and should be tested all the time when it changes, or it might depend some brand-spanking new code that doesn’t quite work and you know it doesn’t work yet but you want the test system to be less tedious.

What else looks like this?

  • CircleCI
  • Snap CI by ThoughtWorks

Old code and old tools

One of the problems with old codebases is that they are missing modern tooling for automated builds, automated testing, and the like.

A second problem with old code is that the maintainers (absent modern tooling) may not know or recognize what dependencies they have.

If an old package is not mostly self-contained, it’s likely to have dependencies on other old code. This makes it hard to build, as those older dependencies have either gone offline or have mutated so that the old package doesn’t work with the new releases.

To confound the matter even more, the newer tools for automated builds, automated tests and so forth have their own sets of complex dependencies, so much so that unless you are around some team that has already absorbed these systems that you are unlikely to grasp all of their deeply interconnected complexities.

It’s easy to cascade into a deep chasm of interlocking dependencies, yielding enough complexity in the build process to frustrated people who really care more about the code than how it’s packaged for distribution.

I’ve been working this weekend on tidy-html5. It has a Makefile that’s generated by CMake, and its documentation is built with a combination of xsltproc and Doxygen. As dependencies go that’s not too bad, but the problem starts because the codebase lay dormant for a couple of years, and somewhere between three and six years of net development and build tooling efforts have passed it by.

There was no Dockerfile for the project, so I wrote one to see if I could get it to build and could understand all of the build dependencies. That took a dozen tries, but eventually something went through to completion.

The first thing I noted was that it not only depended on CMake but also a particularly relatively recent version of CMake. Ditto for Doxygen - not any elderly Doxygen will do, but only a relatively recent build. Doxygen has its own dependencies, lots of them - about a gigabyte of code to download and install.

Next was to pick up on a previous effort to build the whole thing under Travis CI. Now I have a relatively old build system that doesn’t have these old dependencies so I have to drag them in, and not only drag in some version but a particularly new version.

Dependencies satisfied, it’s time to build. I want this to work on my Raspberry Pi, so I build it there, and discover that the version of Doxygen there is too old (oops) so it doesn’t build the docs at all, never mind the cheery message from the build script that claims “TidyLib API documentation has been built”.

Next, to test. Does it pass all of its tests? Well, there is a test directory with a bunch of test cases, but the documentation says things like

Of course some of the tests were to say avoid a segfault found. Other tests were to visually compare the original input test file in a browser, with how the new output displayed in a browser. This is a purely VISUAL compare, and can not be done in code.

Frankly, a test that can’t be done in code is one that I can’t run in finite time. I build all of the tests, diff them with the reference directory, and hope that the differences (there are a few) aren’t fatal.

You see where this is going…

Frankly, it’s hard to get all of the tooling right. Travis CI has all of its own quirks, and you have to set up a build correctly to get correct build completion results. Once the build happens, you want some sort of test harness to take all of these test cases and turn them into red lights and green lights on your screen, and I don’t have that yet. Building binaries is yet another task - one project contributor put together a Jenkins install to take on that task. And so forth.

The piece of this that I care about is twofold. tidy as it stands now is frozen in 2009, and that’s before HTML5. tidy-html5 is caught in flux, with a successful project rescue to get it to build at all, but not yet the full-on automation and test infrastructure that projects like docker or node.js have that allow for ferocious parallel development with reasonable test and integration coverage.

The tidy project is very old (20 yrs), and if it’s going to pass a full suite of automated tests on every check-in for every platform, that’s going to take some consolidated effort - both by people who care about the code (but not so much how it’s packaged) and by people who care about the packaging (so that they can use the code). Not only does the code need to evolve, but the build tools around the code need to move forward.

10 September 2015

xml2json as part of a web parsing pipeline

The problem, simply put. You have a messy, real world HTML page; you want to parse it to pull out some key element of it, to pass along to some other task; you want that parsing pipeline to be as compact as possible, because you’re running on a small machine, and you’re doing the task frequently, and because the page may change out from under you at any time.

Clearly this is not an optimal task to have, but you live with the web you have, not with the web you want to have.

My approach to this task is to assemble a pipeline of Unix tools that are as simple as possible, and that each do one thing pretty well, and that in combination are all well-refined enough that there are few surprises.

curl is the workhorse for fetching pages. The invocation curl -m 15 -s http://example.com pulls that page from the net and feeds it to standard output, but times out after 15 seconds so your pipeline doesn’t completely fail if a remote site is down.

tidy is the first thing I look at when doing data transformation. tidy -q -asxml 2>/dev/null takes HTML and converts it, quietly and uncomplainingly, into XML. It’s predictable and pretty fast. tidy has been around since the dawn of the web, and it newly has a Github project and a shiny web page and a support consortium, so if you want to build from source it’s readily possible.

Given XML, convert it to JSON. Here there are quite a few choices, depending on which language and which parser you want to start with, and the dependency tree is deep. I am least satisfied with my alternatives here, thus the motivation for this writeup. In alphabetical order by language:

There is no POSIX standard xml2json command written in C, alas.

In C++ there’s an xml2json command and library from Cheedoong Ch’ng. The Github project says

xml2json is the first carefully written C++ library that converts XML document to JSON format. It’s already been used in the soft subtitle cross-domain solution at the server-end of Tencent Video (http://v.qq.com) and its CDNs.

In Dart, see Steve Hamblett’s xml2json.dart library. The Github project includes a set of unit tests, as well as explicit support for three conventions (Parker, Badgerfish, and Google Data) for doing the conversion. The test suites are welcomed.

In Go, see Darren Elwood (textnode)’s xml2json.go. The Github project includes sample code to parse both RSS and generic XML files.

Transform a stream of XML into a stream of JSON, without requiring a schema or structs, written in Go (golang.org)

In Node.JS, see node-xml2json from BugLabs which converts XML to JSON using node-expat. You can install it with npm install xml2json.

In Perl, see Isidro Vila Verde’s xml2json.pl, which has a Github project and is based on XSLT. “You may need to install some perl modules before using it” is the extent of the install instructions.

In Python, see the xmlutils package from Kailash Nadh and Yigal Lazarev. You can install it with pip install xmlutils, or look at the Github project.

xmlutils.py is a set of Python utilities for processing xml files serially for converting them to various formats (SQL, CSV, JSON). The scripts use ElementTree.iterparse() to iterate through nodes in an XML document, thus not needing to load the entire DOM into memory. The scripts can be used to churn through large XML files (albeit taking long :P) without memory hiccups.

Once the original HTML document emerges nicely formatted as JSON, it’s relatively easy to pick out elements from it in most cases. Two tools to do this are jq and jp.

jq, from Stephen Dolan, is a command-line JSON processor. Now at version 1.5, it has a web site and a Github repository, plus a handy online test site that lets you do interactive tests. It’s written in portable C.

jq is like sed for JSON data - you can use it to slice and filter and map and transform structured data with the same ease that sed, awk, grep and friends let you play with text.

jp, from James Saryerwinnie, is a command line version of the JMESPath query language for JSON, documented at http://jmespath.org/ . This is the same query language embedded into the Amazon Web Services command line (AWS CLI), and it has powerful and compact operators for extracting elements from a JSON document. There are libraries in Python, PHP, Javascript, Ruby, Lua, and Go that implement JMESPath. jp is written in Go, and has cross-development tools available.

So that’s the environment. It’s a bit of a pain to get all of those tools running on a new bare-metal machine, so my thought is to put everything into one Docker build and make it straightforward to wrap everything together. Ideally this will be a fairly minimalist build, and thus the challenge is to find the set of dependencies that is small enough.

Deep web

I think this whole “deep web” thing is mis-stated for the most part.

Since forever there have been Internet-based services that have been unreachable to the public unless you had some kind of specialized access. Private internal networks are the norm at companies of any size. Calling that the “hidden web”, “deep web” is non-sensical in that it’s not really hidden to anyone who has proper access to it - it’s just that you (and Google) don’t and won’t have that access.

Furthermore there are enormous numbers of home networks behind NAT devices where there are home servers that are either accidentally or deliberately off-limits. If you want to get that data onto the public network you actually have to do some work to make it happen.

I even have a batch of test services running on my laptop that don’t have any public presence at all beyond my keyboard.

There happen to be parts of the “secret web”, “deep web” etc where there’s some naughty content, but for the most part the Internet architecture makes it completely normal to have hosts that only have partial routing to the world, and most of the time that’s completely innocuous.

The challenge is that there’s a lot of data out there that changes all the time, and you want to gather it, make sure it’s sane, squirrel it away for later analysis, put together a pretty chart so that you can spot check it, feed it into other systems not yet fully dreamed of to cause actions, and so on. Phase one of this effort was wrestling MongoDB into service, which ended up being only a handful of lines of code; but the concern expressed was the Mongo though fun and easy for small projects gets messy for big ones, and there’s good reason not to succumb to its siren song. Phase two is a look at RRDtool, a “round robin” database very often used for network management efforts. With that in mind you can have a single database with 10 years worth of daily averages and a whole year of minute-by-minute data and it hardly takes up any room at all - perfect for the sort of “data logger” applications that I find myself in the middle of.

Using Github Issues and Waffle.io as a personal kanban board

So the challenge is coming up with a simple configuration of the system that doesn’t ignore what might be done, but that focuses on what really needs to be done.

The inspriation for all of this is Jim Benson’s “Personal Kanban”, and his observation that really the task simplifies down to two rules:

  • Limit work in progress
  • Visualize your work
