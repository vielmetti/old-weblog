3 August 2015

Michigan Emergency Incidents and overnight storm report

Michigan Emergency Incidents is a news service that covers emergency dispatch around the state of Michigan. Members of a closed Facebook group post call-outs of emergency services operations, and there’s generally follow up from other members to note incidents in progress or news stories written from the scene.

All information contained in MEI posts is to come from received radio transmissions heard directly from radios / scanners or via an online broadcast, verified reliable sources, or first hand knowledge (ie you are on scene).

Follow along on Twitter @MEIDispatch; the twitter feed has the current link to the private Facebook group, which regularly accepts qualified new members.

Storm last night. We got a downpour, some wind and lightning, but nothing too bad. A nearby City of Ann Arbor rain gauge on S Industrial reported 1.12” of rain, and at the peak of the storm rain was coming down at 0.41” in 15 minutes. The scanner was full of power lines sparking and trees down.

Worst hit was the Sleeping Bear Dunes area, where some of the adjacent communities had complete power blackouts. There was also a tornado in the Thumb. The National Weather Service recap for the August 2, 2015 storms gives much more detail. Softball sized hail was seen near West Branch, MI.

Current 8:45 a.m. report is 60,318 DTE Energy customers without power, and 91,802 Consumers Energy customers without power. DTE does not generally report outages by county, just by zip code, but I have all of the zip codes so I should be able to estimate county level totals.

2 August 2015

Blogrolls

A blogroll is a list of other weblogs that the writer links to, in the hopes that they will get a link back in return. It’s one way to establish yourself as part of a community of writers.

The practice of keeping and maintaining a blogroll is completely gone, as self-linking networks like Facebook and Twitter have obviated the need for explicitly calling out those linkages. Our self-updating feeds simply stream a continuous loop of content into our willing eyeballs, interspersed with as many advertisements as we will stand.

Still, blogrolls are useful as a quaint reminder of times past, and how we once organized our reading in the days before efficient newsfeeds became the rule of the day. Look at the bottom of the far left of this blog for mine.

1 August 2015

Knolling

Knolling is the practice of organizing things neatly, especially to arrange things in parallel lines or at right angles.

Thanks to Jason Kottke for the always be knolling idea and background info, which he attributes to Andrew Kromelow.

For some inspriation see Things Organized Neatly, a Tumblr from Austin Radcliffe.

More: Hannah Sitzer, Always Be “Knolling”—My New Favorite Word, Inspired by Tom Sachs, 2011.

A Tom Sachs movie, “Always be knolling”, from Ten Bullets

Previously spotted in a similar way, the “5S” process of getting rid of clutter, especially “seiton” or “neatness”. Everything goes into its own place. Knolling is a bit more freeform and does away with explicit labels.

It does get me to thinking thought that I’m very tired of some of the presentation of screens that I look at all the time. My Gmail inbox is always a simple list; would it not be that hard some time to make it a grid of squares with people’s photos in them, so that I could see at a glance who was in my inbox?

Once you start to sort one thing, you want to sort everything.

31 July 2015

mitmproxy

In his An internet x-ray machine for the masses, Paul Ohm writes a lucid introduction to mitmproxy, a man-in-the-middle proxy server that allows you to get between your browser and the Internet and poke at (and change) the traffic going between the two.

http://mitmproxy.org is the home of mitmproxy. It’s the work of Aldo Cortesi and a cast of hundreds of contributors on github at https://github.com/mitmproxy/mitmproxy . I used the OS X binary distribution from the download page. There’s also a nifty Dockerized version on Dockerhub as docker pull mitmproxy/mitmproxy to get you started quickly too.

Man-in-the-middle (or monkey-in-the-middle) systems are not new, of course, and Dug Song’s dsniff package dates to 1999. What we’ve learned in a decade and a half is that security through obscurity vanishes in the face of better tools for looking at network traffic as it’s going by with high level tools for making sense of what’s there.

As ever, all of these tools have both benign and malicious uses. If you can trick someone into using your proxy server for their traffic, all kinds of foolishness can result. Better is the use that you proxy your own traffic and behold all of the cookies, beacons, and ads that sneak into your everyday browsing experience.

31 July 2015

Soundcloud, now and in the future

If you have exactly one copy of something and it’s only on Soundcloud, this would be an opportune time to make a backup. That’s always a good idea anyway not to rely on single web servers for anything, but it’s especially notable in the light of these articles.

First, the upbeat Bloomberg Business article of July 10, 2015:

… SoundCloud’s potential is fascinating, and perhaps unique. The founders have ambitious goals that respond to changes in the way music and sound are created and consumed. It’s a beautiful dream. So long as all the crucial participants—labels, publishers, artists, SoundCloud itself—can agree on how to share it.

Next, the downbeat Tech Times on July 29, 2015:

We recently reported on rumors that popular music streaming service SoundCloud is planning on adding a subscription-based tier to its currently all-free streaming service, and that rumor was just confirmed by company co-founder Eric Wahlforss in a conversation with the Wall Street Journal. Now, it appears that the impetus behind the development of the subscription tier is clear: the company is in dire need of cash.

“Dire need of cash” because record labels are not thrilled with the beautiful dream of listening to the world’s music for free. Remember how Napster turned out? It was a beautiful dream, too, but labels and publishers just saw it as piracy. This from EDM Tunes:

The 2015 SoundCloud controversy is Napster all over again. We aren’t sharing files anymore, we’re sharing sound. 15 years ago record labels objected to us downloading their music, now they object to us streaming it….

Like Napster, SoundCloud is a goldfish that’s been unwillingly thrust into battle with three super-wale companies. Just for a matter of reference, consider that SoundCloud has about 200 employees, while the Sony, Universal, and Warner corporate networks employ more than 200,000. At this point, SoundCloud is fighting to survive, and unfortunately has received a lot of critical media attention. Since its 2007 inception, SoundCloud has served as a haven for electronic music producers and fans; allowing sound to be seamlessly shared from artist to listener. This luxury is being threatened, and we’re all pissed about it.

So, enjoy Soundcloud while you can, and when you are producing things for it make sure you keep a backup on your own machines!

Tomatillo

I bought a little box of tomatillos from Argus Farm Stop. The question now is what to do with them.

Suggestions so far include:

  • fried like green tomatoes, slice thin, coat with a thin batter
  • roasted them with onions and jalapenos and make into a salsa
  • raw tomatillo salsa a la Rick Bayless
  • fried with onions and then add to scrambled eggs for tortillas
  • baked shrimp with tomatillos, feta, and cilantro
  • carne en su jugo, a la La Fiesta Mexicana in Ypsilanti
  • in pastel de Montezuma

What I ended up making was a very simplified version of Tomatillo Breakfast Tacos, reducing the recipe to just onions, tomatillos, and eggs. Cook chopped onions and chopped tomatillos over medium heat (6 of 10) until the tomatillos are soft and the onions are browned, about 12 minutes. Crack some eggs over the top of the mixture and cover. Cook without stirring until the eggs are set to your desired doneness.

More discussion and commentary on Facebook. Note that Twitter did not supply a good stream of answers, but it did unearth the one recipe I actually used, so there’s that.

25 July 2015

make tidy

I’ve been converting over old postings on this blog from Typepad. The data export and import was imperfect, and this meant that I’ve been poring over the data to figure out what keeps and what goes and what-not. It’s a chance to review old words.

One of the artifacts of the old hosting platform was a helpful at times system called Zemanta. Zemanta would figure out what you were writing about and give you helpful suggestions for related links, both to things that you had written as well as to things other people had written.

I’ve decided as part of the transformation of the old weblog into the new one to unlink those “related links”, in the hope that eventually Hugo’s own system will create some either algorithmically derived relations or at least an easier way to do that by hand.

In the meantime I’m looking closely at what Zemanta did so I can undo it. The first set of artifacts is a “fieldset” with its links, and that’s easy enough to search and destroy. Occasionally but not always I find my links decorated with a class="zem_slink" marker that’s a little harder to find. Most insidious so far was exactly one time where Zemanta (or Typepad) rewrote or wrote an Amazon link to include an affiliate link points to Zemanta, and not to me.

Rewriting 1500+ pages is going to take a while. The biggest question is unpublishing some of them, since time has passed and I don’t need to republish everything. There’s conversion from HTML to Markdown to remove the uglies, spelling errors to catch, next categories and taxonomies to fill, layout to work on, etc. At some point I need to sort out image management. But first I’m going to understand the consequences of previous convenience decisions I made to use Zemanta and to undo that decision.

A few months ago I wrote a tiny R script that generates maps. The challenge today is to find a portable environment so that I can deploy this script on shared infrastructure. The project took 30 minutes of MacBook Air processor time running full blast parallel, and while I don’t care about wall clock time to complete the effort I do care about building something that I can hand off to someone else to run.

The hope is that the ropensci Docker package from the Rocker project is the answer, or most of the answer, for this. Rocker has built R images where lots of useful packages have been installed, including systems where the install directions are a little bit complex.

With all complicated software environments the biggest challenge is dealing with dependencies. If you want to use the latest and greatest fast algorithms, you have to be prepared to spin up on what the cool kids are doing in the rest of their environment. If somehow you can jump to the edge of the development in the algorithm world without being tripped up by the hairy dependency graph that got you there, it can be a lot more fun.

So; install Docker on the Mac with ‘boot2docker’; then

docker pull rocker/ropensci
docker run -it rocker/ropensci R

and poof, you have an environment current to this month’s desktop of leading R developers.

22 July 2015

Starting a trial of Little Bird

Little Bird is a system that helps you discover influencers and experts in any topic. I’m at the very start of a 7 day trial. Marshall Kirkpatrick is CEO of Little Bird, and he has been active on Twitter posting interesting ideas about influence marketing - and given that it seemed prudent to give the product a try.

Pricing is aimed at agencies, not individuals, so I’m going to look at this product through the eyes of people doing “social listening” for a living, tracking the messages that appear on a topic and either quietly making sense of what is happening or actively engaging with those people to try to influence their opinions. The single user price is out of my reach for an annual commitment, though I might keep it up for another month if I can find a specific task that warrants the extra tooling.

My first test with any social software is to point it at a domain that I know (Ann Arbor) and see what it unearths. Little Bird found for me 1000 influential accounts having to do with Ann Arbor, and in a boost to my ego it puts me in the top 50. Without understanding how the algorithms work it seems that it biases towards identifying busy accounts that post a lot of news, vs. the clever individual whose every post is good but who doesn’t post that often.

There’s a style of posting which I would have to describe as “thinkfluential” which Little Bird has a nose for. The thinkfluencer will post frequently on a given topic, and a lot of people will retweet them and they will retweet a lot of people. Every message is relentlessly on message and you won’t find cat pictures or meeting banter mixed in. More often than not you’ll see the thinkfluence augmented by robots that post newsy things day and night, even when the thinkfluencer is away from the keyboard. There doesn’t even need to be any interaction at all with this “expert”, just a never-ending confluence of thinkfluence.

So I wish that Little Bird had another round of filters, to help me understand the question at scale “is this person really a bot”. The real utility for me of identifying expertise is not to pick out the high-frequency posting bot, but to suss out who on a topic is good at engaging on the things they know about. Bot identification is the Turing Test at work, and maybe just maybe there’s a set of computable metrics that you can build across a corpus of Twitter users to pull that out.

Library notes from Malletts Creek

Out for a bike ride to Malletts Creek Branch Library after a delicious lunch of BBQ at Ricewood.

One of the challenges of low overhead custom postcard making is getting an image manipulation pipeline that kicks out photos that a appropriately cropped and sized. The latest challenge is identifying the single command line that will turn a photo from my phone’s camera into an appropriately sized image for the card with a minimum of cropping, distortion, and manual file transfers.

What I know doesn’t work is upload via Twitter, because the series of resizings generate visible compression artifacts.

I can upload from the phone to OneDrive, which gives me a photo of size 2592 × 1456 pixels (1.78:1, 16:9). The target size is 1875 x 1275 (1.47:1, 25:17), which is postcard format. I can keep all of the pixels in the image by letterboxing it, or I can crop off the edges. What I can’t really do is stretch the image to fit because it’s too far off.

Looking further at my camera software (Lumia Camera) it appears that I can also take photos at 4:3. There’s more experimenting to be done.

For more documentation, see Efficient Image Resizing With ImageMagick, Dave Newton in Smashing Magazine, June 25, 2015. Dave goes through a professionally thorough view of resizing and resampling images for the web, comparing his results against Photoshop. For instance:

I tested 31 different settings for -filter and got the best results with Triangle. The Triangle resampling filter is also known as bilinear interpolation, which I discussed above. It determines pixel color by looking at a support area of neighboring pixels and produces a weighted average of their colors. I found it best to specify this support area at two pixels using the -define filter:support=2 setting.

Dave uses DSSIM from https://pornel.net/dssim to test his results.

This tool computes (dis)similarity between two PNG images using (my approximation of) algorithms approximating human vision. Comparison is done in La*b color space using a multi-scale variant of the SSIM algorithm.

There are sites that have automatic crop algorithms, though I haven’t yet found a package that’s usable without licensing. The interactive web site of Croppola is one useful example of this genre.

The issue that 1.7.1 resolves for me is certificate support - if I understand the bug right, the Docker daemon was creating a key for each interface it had, but not all of the interfaces were up at key init time. The fix was to move DHCP init earlier in the start sequence so that everything was initialized by the time key creation took place.

The Docker team is trying to improve code quality by having their code base pass the golint test. The open issue, fix golint errors/warnings #14784, has attracted a swarm of contributors, and a lot of them have proposed changes that don’t pass the automated tests. The build farm is managed with Jenkins, and you can see the status of every build of Docker on their dashboard.

Having a full lintable codebase is a great goal. The challenge comes when the suggested changes by lint send you in the direction of making an incompatible change to some interface that has the “wrong” name but that people were relying on.

Perhaps importantly for the work I’m doing it’s worth noting that currently all of these tests are done on x86 platforms (Linux and Windows) and there isn’t an official automated build on ARM. I’ve been using the Docker distribution from Hypriot as my baseline for working on the Raspberry Pi, with good success.

bocker is Docker (or at least a good sized subset of it) in 100 lines of shell script. I suppose the point is that even though there’s a huge world of hype surrounding containerization as a next step of systems management, when you get down to it, it’s just not that complicated.

The work of the morning is finishing up yesterday’s work on sending postcards with Lob.com. Key to this effort is resizing your favorite images so that they meet the template. Fortunately I had the presence of mind seven years ago to write up my notes on using sips and Imagemagick for image manipulation and that helped. In this case the magic incantation was

sips -z 1275 1875 original.jpg --out new.jpg

If you don’t have a Mac, or want to do this on a server or container somewhere, you’ll want Imagemagick. That venerable program has a bazillion options, and you’ll find the image geometry options at http://www.imagemagick.org/script/command-line-processing.php#geometry . Recreating this exact sips command is left as an exercise for the reader.

It remains only to tally up these PostGutenberg values: If the world’s esoteric scholarly/scientific literature were available to everyone for free in electronic form, the first benefit to the author would be the great increase in the visibility, accessibility and hence the potential impact of his work (1). This would of course also be a benefit to all scholars when they are wearing their readers’ rather than their authors’ hats (2). Some fear that such a literature would be overwhelming and un-navigable, but stop and think: How do we currently manage it in paper? If the entire corpus were transferred to the Net, instead of our eyes and fingers and feet doing the walking to get to the papers or to get the papers to us, electronic directories containing everything could be searched using the kinds of keyword search already used today in searching electronic databases that contain the titles and abstracts (but not the articles) in the paper literature. I first ran into the “pocketmod” planning technique in 2005, and here it is 10 years later and there’s one in my pocket.

A pocketmod is a cleverly folded piece of paper with preprinted templates for things like a mini calendar, todo list, quadrille paper, checklist etc. It’s the smallest instance of a PDA that I have seen yet, requiring nothing more than a blank piece of paper and a pair of scissors to execute. You could even bypass the scissors if you had good paper and a crisp straightedge to do a rip.

Astoundingly, http://www.pocketmod.com/ is still online, in all of its 2005-2007 glory. That system does an 8-up PDF from a set of templates, so that you can print a lovingly detailed mini booklet. My own use has long since devolved into scrap paper plus rip down the center. When it fills up, invert the fold, and you have a brand new organizer.

lob a postcard: printer sends cards via API

Lob.com has an API to send postcards-as-a-service ($0.70 per each, full custom both sides). Details at https://lob.com/services/postcards . It works, but all of the user interface is up to you; if you know a good service for delivering a single full-custom postcard with postage that actually has a user interface and not an API for less than $1.00 each, let me know and I’ll update here.

I’ve started checking in a few tiny utility programs that exercise the Lob API. Look for https://github.com/vielmetti/postcard on Github. The goal at some point is to have a “postcard” application that has a user interface like so.

postcard --to mom --photo cute-kids.jpg --message "they get so big so fast! love ed"

Also in this business is PostalMethods with a Postcard API based on SOAP. That’s not a convenient interface in the languages that I use, but you can look at the API at http://www.postalmethods.com/lp/postcard-api . Their prices for postcards run $1.50 per each or less, with quantity discounts - see http://www.postalmethods.com/pricing for details.

Postful used to have an API for postcards. They were bought by Click2Mail in 2011. Pricing for Click2Mail’s postcards starts at $0.25 per card plus $0.284 postcard, but there’s a $2.00 minimum if you don’t have credits on account. I wasn’t able to determine at first glance whether their REST API applied to onesy-twoesy printing options, though it was clear that if you wanted to send 1000 cards that the pricing would be competitive.
