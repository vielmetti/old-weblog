lob a postcard: printer sends cards via API

Lob.com has an API to send postcards-as-a-service ($0.70 per each, full custom both sides). Details at https://lob.com/services/postcards . It works, but all of the user interface is up to you; if you know a good service for delivering a single full-custom postcard with postage that actually has a user interface and not an API for less than $1.00 each, let me know and I’ll update here.

Also in this business is PostalMethods with a Postcard API based on SOAP. That’s not a convenient interface in the languages that I use, but you can look at the API at http://www.postalmethods.com/lp/postcard-api . Their prices for postcards run $1.50 per each or less, with quantity discounts - see http://www.postalmethods.com/pricing for details.

Postful used to have an API for postcards. They were bought by Click2Mail in 2011. Pricing for Click2Mail’s postcards starts at $0.25 per card plus $0.284 postcard, but there’s a $2.00 minimum if you don’t have credits on account. I wasn’t able to determine at first glance whether their REST API applied to onesy-twoesy printing options, though it was clear that if you wanted to send 1000 cards that the pricing would be competitive.

The weak ties of your social network are its greatest strength

Lior Zoref has a new book out called Mindsharing: the art of crowdsourcing everything (Portfolio / Penguin, 2015). I have a copy of it from the Ann Arbor District Library which was on the new shelf at Mallett’s Creek on a sunny and hot summer Sunday afternoon. Mallett’s has good air conditioning, a very good spot to be on a warm day.

The challenge of a book like this is picking out the good bits without repeating the author’s technique of using the word “Mindsharing” (capitalized) at every opportunity. I’ll try to unpack the approach with a simpler version of the strategy, which seems to boil down to this:

  • Make lots of connections online, even with people who you don’t know very well
  • Ask them really good questions when you are trying to make a decision

Lots of connections is the biggest suggestion here; rather than get help from a small circle of trusted friends, the crowdsourcing approach to human improvement relies on good ideas coming from large or very large sets of people (at least 250), and relies on pulling in unexpectedly good recommendations from people who don’t know you very well. Lior references Mark Granovetter’s “The Strength of Weak Ties” (1973) early on, but of course Granovetter didn’t know about Quora, Facebook, LinkedIn, and the host of other online properties and lists that allow for computer-mediated interaction with people at a distance.

There is an art to asking good questions that could fill a book. If you look on the biggest question-answering websites like Quora or Stack Exchange and start to follow along, you will quickly find that good questions get rewarded with positive feedback and bad questions (or impossible to answer questions) get ignored or criticized. Lior points out the there’s a very big difference between typing a query into a search engine and asking a live audience the same thing - the human element of the search allows a personal connection.

Lior is known for a TED talk (he brought an ox onstage) and is out promoting his book. The book itself feels like an ideal airplane read for someone who is aspiring to self-improvement, is facing a career change, and who hasn’t figured out what the point is quite yet with Facebook as a career tool.

Notes on the Midland G-227 GMRS/FRS radio

Midland G-227 GMRS radio, quantity two, a gift from the Wells-Becker Motorcycle Emporium in Dexter, Michigan.

I replaced batteries in both of them, they take 4xAAA.

Radio 1 successfully hears static when the (M) monitor button is depresssed; Radio 2 hears nothing.

A manual is at http://www.manualslib.com/manual/396326/Midland-G-225.html which describes the frequencies of the 22 channels it can transmit on. A reasonable next step is to get my NooElec SDR tuner out and see if I can monitor a transmission. If Radio 2 transmits, I suspect a loose connection to the speaker.

In the same gift also a Uniden labelled “Scramble” that has a NOAA weather logo on it, so it probably also monitors weather frequencies.

You can never have too many radios!

food coop notes for July 18, 2015

Some notes over a cup of coffee and some cold roast chicken. I need to mix up my menu selections at the coop more so that I can remember what I was writing by what I ate; I usually eat the same thing!

Peter Honeyman and Lynn Chamberlain were here at the coop, doing the NY Times crossword. (Saturday crossword in 36:05, a mid-day triumph.)

At Farmer’s Market: cherries, parsley, kale, potatoes, dill, tomatoes. The potatoes will be boiled and dressed with a dill vinaigrette. (I had to look up the word vinaigrette.) The recipes I found add green beans and boiled eggs to a salad with cold potatoes, using a mustard vinaigrette with added chopped dill. The cherries will probably just be eaten out of hand.

The UP travel discussion included the obligatory discussion of insects, including ticks (“like bedbugs, for the woods”) and horrible sand flies that will swarm you and leave you screaming running from the beach. There’s a good reason that the UP has miles of undeveloped beaches.

You can’t mention bugs in the UP without remembering The Backyard Arthropod Project, “A Field Guide to the North Side of Old Mill Hill, Atlantic Mine, MI”, by Tim Eisele.

I’m still hacking away at generating interesting reports from the stringdist package from Mark van der Loo. Yesterday’s post was R: producing a list of near matches with stringdist and stringdistmatrix and the answers provided helped unearth the melt() function from the reshape2 library, and with that you take a matrix and turn it into an edge list. A little bit of filtering, and you get a report of near matches in a list, enough for me to catch variant spellings of vinagrette to vinaigrette in a “make test” step.

Stack Overflow was fun for this task, I’m going to try it again. The process of describing your problem in enough detail that someone who knows the issue can add the one or two lines of code to make it work is a useful exercise. It’s the opposite of a search engine in many ways - of course I could find melt() once I know its name, but if you don’t know the name you can’t find anything.

The next bit of interestingness has to do with turning the dist data structure returned by stringdist into a variety of useful images. There’s a package that dumps out a “dendogram”, and if I’m careful in formatting the data I think I can coerce R into producing data that can be imported directly into the tools that Valdis Krebs has written for network exploration and visualization. The promise within R is a “distgraph” that does the visualization, but I’m not yet finding the cadre of experts who use that.

The art on the walls is Julie Marron-Parker’s “Sunburst”. She writes

Let the sun shine.

Living in Michigan (160 days of sun per year) I have come to appreciate when the sun is out. This piece brings the energy of the sun into the world, regardless of the weather outside.

Tom Meloche writes about the doorway effect, where going through a doorway wipes out your short term memory to make way for new short term memories. It got me thinking about the memory palace , and how you might construct in your mind a series of portals and doorways to walk through so that you can remember a series of items in such a way that you take advantage of the doorway effect to pull memories in and out of your short term thinking. The technology is “mnemotechnics”, that’s a good keyword to pull lots of interesting thinking (which sometimes sounds like crazytalk).

That reminds me - I need to move to another room, so that I can remember what I need to do here.

Levenshtein distance for approximate match

The problem, simply stated, is that of typoes and inconsistent spelling and pluraliziation. Let’s say you have a collection of text that you want to annotate with some metadata, like categories and tags. You don’t want to set aside ahead of time exactly which tags you want to use, but you want to catch through some test or lint step that you’ve done something wrong by typing “apples” into a field that really has “apple” most of the time. A good algorithm will catch minor deviations from the norm and advise you when you should go back with a fresh pair of eyes and do an edit. The bigger problem is deduplication .

My first pass at a proposed solution was to look for a version of the old Unix program “uniq” that would have an approximate match. There is also an old Unix program “agrep” (“approximate grep”) from Udi Manber.

[WM92a] Wu S. and U. Manber, “Agrep - A Fast Approximate Pattern-Matching Tool,” Usenix Winter 1992 Technical Conference, San Francisco (January 1992), pp. 153-162.

Neither of these first lines of attack to see if someone had completely solved the problem before did the trick, but some searching around and a conversation with John Hritz hit the neuron that remembered the algorithm called the Levenshtein distance . That led me to Wikibook: Algorithm Implementation , and its set of Levenshtein distance code. It so happened that there was a very slow implementation in the bash shell included in that collection that was a cut and paste and two lines of code from being exactly what I needed. A little bit of glue code in awk and I was ready to go.

Now the problem with cut and paste two lines of code is that this particular implmentation is very slow, albeit correct. Given the choice between slow correct code and fast code that’s wrong (because of the bug that you don’t yet understand), I’ll take the slow code plus a comment and a writeup that describes how to make it faster.

What I’m really after is just the pairwise comparison of a set of words where the two words are close to each other. If the distance between two words is greater than some threshhold, I don’t need to go any further. That algorithmic shortcut isn’t in the library codes that I found.

The bigger question is how to write a checklist and implement it in code to make sure that a big body of work stays internally consistent. It’s one thing to have a categorization rule for your writers that says “always use the singular”, but very much something different to have Test 38 in your automated test corpus apply that rule and notice when it’s wrong.

valuable archives or just fishwrap

You’re writing away, and you hit “publish”, and the thing you pieced together flings its way through the ether to some audience, real or imagined. The next day it’s nearly impossible to find again unless you manage to produce the precise incantation into a search engine. There is some long-lasting trail, to be sure, easy enough to dredge up if someone is motivated by money or curiousity, but for the most part the social networks of today (Facebook, Twitter) generate most of their energy from capturing the attention of the moment.

Some of what I write here is worthwhile to save for longer than a day, and some would be best to be deleted and never seen again. I’m happy though that I have it all in one place that I can grep through, happy that I can make changes to it easily enough, and happy that it can be deleted or unpublished without much fuss.

custom views of taxonomies

One of the motivation ideas behind going to the work to move a big weblog to a new platform is to get better control over categories. With Typepad, every category looks about the same; the idea now is with Hugo I can have separate categories that look different. For instance:

There are a number of recipes in the collection, which should be sorted alphabetically or by key ingredient, not chronologically.

There are a number of posts about books in the collection, which should be connected to a lookup for that book in your favorite bookstore or my favorite library.

There are a number of people mentioned, and a people index would be great so you can browse and not just search.

Mostly I’m after improving the browsing experience, as much for me as for anyone else. If you have to find things with a search engine, you are stuck in a world where you have to know that something has a particular name. By offering a browsing infrastructure first, you cherrypick the best search terms and put them up front.

Some of this will need pen and paper design….

What do I want to have happen as a result of all of this not inconsiderable effort? Creative control to be sure, to be able to type something and see it appear exactly as desired on the screen. Community engagement is another, to be part of a group of people all working toward a common goal.

Publishing from Hugo to Github Pages

One of the challenges in getting things started is figuring out where the end product is going to live. Hugo has its own web server which is pretty nice, but since it generates static pages, the results can really go anywhere.

This tutorial goes through the process in excruciating detail

http://gohugo.io/tutorials/github-pages-blog/

but somehow fails to make it easy. What I’d like is a publishing workflow that just like the normal Hugo workflow puts things online instantly once they are built.

This one deploys with CodeShip

http://jice.lavocat.name/blog/2015/hugo-deployment-via-codeship/

And this one deploys with Wercker

http://gohugo.io/tutorials/automated-deployments/

Yet one more, with Github hooks and S3/Cloudfront; it depends on s3cmd.

https://github.com/imperialwicket/imperialwicket.com

Finally, one that focuses on the github side of things:

https://gist.github.com/chrisjacob/825950

The approach I have runs like so, it assumes that you have a working directory “~/src/hugo” with your blog in it, and a target directory “~/src/vielmetti.github.io” set up for github pages.

cd ~/src/hugo
hugo -d ../vielmetti.github.io --theme=vacuum
# wait to build, then
cd ../vielmetti.github.io
git add -A .
git commit -m "build complete"
git push origin master

By having separate build directories like this you can keep the continuous build on your draft setup going, and then push a working version whenever you’re perfectly happy with the result. Not like anyone is really perfectly happy with any blog.

Mac and Goat

Mac and Goat calls for goat cheese; we generally use a goat gouda or a goat cheddar, both of which are usually available at the local Trader Joe’s. Feel free to substitute any hard cheese that melts well in your recipe.

The dish also calls for rice pasta, but again feel free to substitute; you’re aiming for something like a rotini which handles the sauce well.

Preheat oven to 375 degrees. Take out a lasagna pan, which is what you’re going to assemble the dish in.

Mac and Goat

  • 1 lb. pasta
  • 1⁄2 lb. goat gouda, goat cheddar, or a nice aged gouda or cheddar
  • 1 large can crushed tomatoes
  • bread crumbs
  • ground nutmeg
  • romano cheese

Boil water for pasta; cook a pound or so of your favorite pasta, we use a rice rotini pasta in this one.

Shred the cheese fine in a food processor or with a hand grater.

When the pasta is done, drain it, and then put about half of it in the lasagna pan. Cover with about half of the cheese and half of the tomatoes. Put the rest of the pasta in, then more tomatoes and more cheese. Top with breadcrumbs and then sprinkle a little romano cheese on top and a few pinches of nutmeg for flavor.

Cook 20-25 minutes, until the top is lightly browned and the tomato sauce is bubbling.

At some point you can iterate endlessly on appearance and completely lose track of contents. Fortuantely I have a body of work that I really want to see in this, so that motivates some level of forward progress. 14 July 2015

converting typepad to hugo

The task of the night was getting a few years worth of Typepad posts into Hugo.

My first step was to convert to Jekyll format, which I did using this typepad_to_jekyll Ruby gem:

https://github.com/koozie/typepad_to_jekyll

You then need to do a few things to get the Jekyll files exactly right for Hugo:

  1. Change the date format
  2. Rewrite the Categories
  3. Download and relink all of the images

Changing the format for “date” was a one-liner in perl with the -i (“edit in place”) flag. I didn’t get to the category rewrite or the image rescue yet. The approach I took was to simply hack on the output files, rather than change the source code to the typepad_to_jekyll program, but if you were serious about it you’d do the latter and fork it until it did the right thing.

Relinking all of the images requires opening a support ticket because Typepad does not have an easy image export. Actually image hosting is a real challenge and I don’t know exactly the right way to do it inside Hugo.

I’ll need to adjust the theme or pick a new theme so that it only shows the first n posts on a page and then paginates, and I also need to adjust it so that it shows categories. The show everything approach is unreasonable when you have 1000s of pages to show off. On the other hand, the most reasonable themes that I can find that look nice don’t have pagination turned on by default. There’s some learning curve to roll my own, maybe one that has some kind of reasonable sidebar structure.

wow that is fast

So 634 pages from my Github Gollum based Wiki markup all got imported into Hugo and it rendered all of them in 828 ms, or basically under a second to pull in everything I’ve ever written. Astonishingly fast.

Alas, those files while they are in Markdown are not really ready for direct import, since they have [[double brackets]] everywhere that get uglified. The other limiting factor is the date marker that’s not included with them so they all show up as January 1, 0001 which is funny the first few times and then not funny.

Doesn’t Hugo have an import function? It does, for converting front matter. The closest for importing other things are the various Jekyll converters which aren’t really the same.

13 July 2015

Bringing up Node-RED and mosquitto under "docker compose" on a Raspberry Pi

Progress, actually quite a lot of it:

The problem, neatly stated. Docker is good for bringing up single services ("microservices"), but sometimes your application needs more than one thing running for the whole thing to work. Enter "docker compose", formerly "fig", which automates the process of running various Docker commands in the right order with the right arguments all driven from a configuration file. As described by their documentation:

Compose is a tool for defining and running complex applications with Docker. With Compose, you define a multi-container application in a single file, then spin your application up in a single command which does everything that needs to be done to get it running.

The particular task I have in front of me is launching Node-RED and mosquitto (an MQTT broker) together on a Raspberry Pi 2 which is running Hypriot and Docker. Most of the background for why that's a relevant task is at this post on Hypriot, How to use Docker Compose to run complex multi container apps on your Raspberry Pi, which I dutifully and successfully managed to run all of the examples from.

Emboldened by this I tried to get Wordpress running with MySQL but got stuck because of a variety of things (not the least of which was some over-enthusiastic documentation that downplayed the complexity of that task, and the lack of a Pi-specific Wordpress Docker images). But having had the taste of what might be I carried on until I managed to get something to work for what I really wanted: Node-RED running alongside mosquitto so that I could have a local MQTT broker to talk to.

nodered:
  image: nieleyde/rpi2-nodered
  command: flows.json
  volumes:
    - /home/pi/node-red:/root/.node-red
  ports: 
    - "1880:1880"
  links:
    - mosquitto

mosquitto:
  image: nieleyde/rpi-mosquitto
  ports:
    - "1883:1883"

Some subtleties to note:

The nieleyde/rpi2-nodered runs on the Pi 2; there's a corresponding nieleyde/rpi-nodered for the Pi. Both are ARM images. I haven't yet tweaked this to run on x86, but if you do, you'll need to update the "image" stanzas everywhere.

The "command" and "volumes" stanzas in the nodered configuration serve to make Docker mount a subdirectory on the host machine where the flows.json file is stored; this makes changes to the configuration persistent. If you want a read-only configuration, you'll need to change this; I haven't gotten that far yet. It might also make sense to have a data volume container, but again I'm not there yet.

The "mosquitto" configuration is extremely bare bones and runs the default config, which is wide open. I haven't gotten far enough to replace the config file with something better, but that's an obvious next step.

From within Node-RED you refer to this internal MQTT server at hostname "mosquitto", port "1883". If the links stanza was changed to "mqtt" and the mosquitto section was changed to "mqtt" you'd then refer to the MQTT server at hostname "mqtt".

For further reading:

An issue open on Niel Eyde's rpi-nodered Github, documentation for how to extend this image with your own nodes and flows, where I'm working some of this out.

On the Node-RED Github, Support for Docker install for Node-RED, which is epic and helpful in many ways.

The essential Hypriot weblog, specifically the aforementioned Docker Compose examples and the Hypriot Docker Compose Github repository.
