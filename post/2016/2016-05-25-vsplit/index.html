Writing in vim with vsplit and multiple windows

I imagine a digital writing environment that mimics how I write in my paper notebooks. On paper I use multiple columns, with parallel writing across the columns and the ability to go back and forth rather than just reading up and down. A recent discussion of a three column writing style describes how and why this works.

An approach new to me is to use very large windows in “vim”, with the “vsplit” command to split the screen into three very tall windows. I don’t manage to get a full 80 column width out of each of them, but 65 columns is close enough that it feels like a workable writing environment. Moving between windows isn’t quite as easy as guiding the pen across the printed page, but it’s much easier than trying to do a fancy layout in a markup language.

It’s difficult to translate the writing environment directly to the screen for display. This post will be on the right two-thirds of the blog with a relatively static left sidebar (which no one ever clicks on, but that seems obligatory for the design). At some point I might do a redesign, and then who knows what the left and right context will be.

There was a brief moment when my writing environment was mostly wiki with special purpose (read: hacked in) commands for maintaining a separate sidebar for each file, displayed adjacent to the main text. This was a personal wiki so I didn’t have to negotiate appropriate activity by any other users. The perpetual connected side display allows you to have a little negotiation with yourself about things that aren’t quite ready to put into the main text.

vim is a very powerful environment, perhaps too powerful. Some key combinations have profound and unexpected power over the command buffer, and you get lost. I find it easier to cope with, though, than the even more powerful emacs. I used GNU Emacs for a while in college but never managed to keep up with it, in large part because the strange key combinations of alt-meta-control-coke-bottle would strain my wrists. At least with vim, I can keep my fingers on the home row for the most part.

Writing context is important, and too often lost. You don’t see the right sidebar where I have notes about the Internet of Things and the sorts of interesting equipment that might mount on a sign post. My notes about a mysterious vim “E212” error in the left sidebar are gone.

It looks like the vsplit windows on this particular screen, when filled, hold about 450 words. Fully loaded, that means that a page properly laid out might have more than 1300 words of usable typing space. I could get used to this layout - not just because it lets me see the whole work, but also because it admits a couple of productive distractions.
