2016 09 05 thinking in graphs vs thinking in lists

Sep 5, 2016 #thinking #graphs

Writing at the Biggby by the dog park with a 20 oz coffee close at hand.

“Thinking style one” comes up with a topic with crisp edges, and then enumerates a set of carefully constrained and largely self-similar elements within that topic. You can be sure at the end that the question at hand has been thoroughly plumbed, and that nothing is missing because you went through diligently to the end.

“Thinking style two” starts with a topic, really anything, and moves laterally and tangentially from one to the next. The cafe reminds you not of other cafes but of the dog park across the street and the converstaions there, but also of the day that you set up a radio at the cafe to listen to airplanes, and that moves tangentially along a trajectory towards radio listening in general.

I can do crisp edges and careful enumeration, but pretty soon I get distracted by the butterfly and then reminded of places in the world that I love where the butterfly lands on milkweed.

Blogging for me has been a process of catching thoughts as they go by, writing them down thoroughly enough that I can reconstruct the mindset at a later date, and then moving on to the next butterfly. Faced with that ephemeral corpus, who can complain that it’s hard to organize? There’s no one overarching theme to anything, just ideas flitting from here to there along a long migration.

When I have had good luck writing on a topic for a long time, it has been the discipline of ensuring that the lead paragraph always had a specific word. I could write anything I wanted to with the words “Ann Arbor” in the lead paragraph for AnnArbor.com; I can write anything with “FOIA” or something similar and get neatly pigeonholed there. But I don’t and won’t write about butterflies often enough to make a “butterfly blog”, even if that’s my style, flitting from one place to another.

NOTES

This Biggby is open until 6pm, even on Labor Day. Meijer is the next stop, for back-to-school lunch shopping.

There’s some ideal size for a collection of posts that are all related - if you can really write 150 separate things that are 500 words each and are all related, you’ve got a substantial body of work. It may make sense to partition my blogging output into several smaller batches and work from that.

One day when listening to airplanes using dump1090 at this Biggby I managed to pick up a plane over Indianapolis. I have a feeling I’ll be buying more SDR radio gear in the near future.

Milkweed grows at Michigania and at the Vielmetti-Peters Conservation Reserve. It likes old fields.

You have to take some care when you have thoughts that go from one idea to the next to explain the transition slowly and clearly for the person listening to you. Sometimes you need to pretend like your mind is grinding slowly and deliberately through a set of predetermined tasks.

Edward Vielmetti
