Halloween Twitter vs Facebook

The two online social networks that consume idle cycles for me are Twitter and Facebook. They really shouldn’t be all that different, but of course they are.

Halloween Twitter for me was full of football, ranging from idle banter about the score to full-on intense #goblue insanity as the game went down to its final moments. (The Team won, 29-26, after The Other Team made a crucial mistake at the end). You didn’t have to have a radio or television to know the score and all of the details.

Halloween Facebook was full of faces of cute kids and sharp-looking costumes and accounts of how many trick-or-treaters showed up at the most popular spots. (1,036 pieces of candy given out, one per kid, at the hotspot).

Twitter works best when there are a lot of people all synchronized on the same event - it’s a remarkably good companion to television or broadcast radio in that way. Unlike the dreams of “Interactive Television” of the 1990s, the broadcast channel and the return channel are completely different, but the effect is the same: the audience is connected to what it’s listening to and to each other.

Facebook doesn’t have that same everyone-is-watching feel, but it makes up for it by biasing the feed that you get towards pictures of faces of friends and family. Even though there’s generally plenty of real-time content to go around, Facebook makes sure you’re not lonely by encouraging you to post more pictures from years gone by.

I’d love to disconnect more from both networks - they absorb time without suitable compensation - and write more elsewhere. We spend too much of our time looking for favorites, thumbs up, and likes on both networks, calibrating the success of our writing by how many random people in the random world clicked a certain button in response. Still they both provide a measure of entertainment.
