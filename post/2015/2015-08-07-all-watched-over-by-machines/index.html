7 August 2015

All watched over by machines

As it turns out, a detailed look at the way you spend time over the course of a day online yields several fascinating conclusions. As previously noted, Wakatime is the introspective tool of choice.

  1. Gee, that’s a lot of time to look at Facebook, Twitter, and Gmail;
  2. Why aren’t you spending more of your productive time writing;
  3. I’ll bet that if you put down the computer your day would be more productive.

Of these, #2 is the most interesting one. Of course time spent on Facebook, Twitter, and Gmail is time spent writing, but it’s transactional writing - short bursts of functional prose to negotiate a time, set up a plan, make a clever comeback or otherwise do online chit-chat. The words are writing, but there’s not a body of work that you can point to that’s of any size as your opus from that hour plus spent.

Productive is a very funny word when you’re being watched by a machine. Some parts of the time are marked as “good time”, where you’re happy to have an extra minute spent credited to a project or task. Not all time is “good time”, though some of the non-good time is just as essential, just measured differently. And then there’s the occasional glitch in the matrix where time is credited or blamed on one thing but you could swear you were doing something else at the time.

Time spent on task sometimes looks very small when all of these are taken into account. Those 99 words you wrote for publication were edited down from 150, and might be edited further down to 55. If you could precisely allocate all of the time you spent on it it would include the musing and ruminations, but that got chalked up to Gmail where it’s mixed in with the time spent deleting mail.

I like to know what I’m doing, but it’s wrong to mix up inputs and outputs. If your system measures inputs, you get ritualized behavior designed to maximize time spent on task, no matter how the time is actually used. Maximizing outputs would yield word counts and not minute counts. If you’re slow and terse, there’s no help for you.
