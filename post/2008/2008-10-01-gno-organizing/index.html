#gno: organizing a network-wide chat on twitter

1 October 2008

#gno: organizing a network-wide chat on twitter

#gno stands for "Girl's Night Out".  It's trending right now on search.twitter.com.  (Yes I am up late; but this is fascinating, I've never seen this particular behavior on twitter before.)

Sometimes you'll see topics trend on search.twitter.com when there are a lot of hipster people in some hipster city at some hipster tech conference.  A zillion people who are all mostly in the same building will use the same tag to post comments on the event.  At this point that's "ordinary".

#gno is different - it's a virtual event, synchronized in time and organized by this loosely affiliated collection of what are charmingly called these days "mommybloggers".  The time sync is important, so that everyone is on the same page, but not everyone knows each other or follows each other on twitter.  This made their first couple of rounds of attempts somewhat frustrating.

The new go-around though seems to be catching on enough that it's a #1 hit (in the twitter universe of 15 minutes of fame), even outpacing the LA earthquake (3.0 magnitude) and perpetual favorite Sarah Palin.  How did they do it?

1.  Loosely coupled networks - blog networks - where there are lots of people linking to each other.  If you had been part of this network you would have known about it from multiple sources.  Like a lot of internet ideas, one way to spread it is to link to all the people who are part of it (example here) and get them to reciprocate.

2.  Tightly synchronized timing - a twitter schedule - where lots of people are asked to show up at a particular time.  The time is key; if you say "some time tonight" you'll get a lot less peak time traffic than if you say "11pm Eastern on the dot".  And while lots of traffic over a long time is interesting, peak traffic is what makes you visible outside your bubble.

3.  A willingness to iterate on tools.  To make this work you need to set some guidelines so that new people joining the network have a recipe to follow to let them be part of it; at the same time, you need to be prepared to switch that recipe from week to week to make it work better or if the tools themselves change.

4.  Someone to be a (nominal or actual) leader who can count noses, decide things, set the network into motion, collect people who are at the fringe and welcome them in, and post the results to improve next time you do it.

5.  Low risk cost of entry into the world (all you have to do is type this tag) and access to a user population who is willing to experiment once on something.

Pretty neat, replicable I suppose.  You should be able to draw parallels from ham radio, where DX contests get a lot of people on the air at once.  There's a similar effect during University of Michigan football games, where when "Michigan" starts to trend you can count on finding other alumni and fans from around the world tuning in.  Television shows synchronize people remarkably well (almost terrifying how well).
