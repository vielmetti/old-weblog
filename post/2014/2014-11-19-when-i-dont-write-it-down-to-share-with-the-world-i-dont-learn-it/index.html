When I don't write it down to share with the world I don't learn it

Nov 19, 2014

I'm working on a project that will remain nameless. The sponsor of the project asked me not to write about it, and I agreed. I'm spending a lot of time communicating about the project, but those communications are all private. It's intensely frustrating.

I learn best by writing about what I'm doing, in enough detail to capture some of the very small bits of what had happened while it was happening. There's no better way for me to learn - and to teach - than by documenting the bits and pieces as I go, while they are still fresh. I write in a form that's framed so that the pieces which are sharable are shared widely, and if there's anything private that gets edited out. I've learned to do this over time, and when it works, it works well.

When you write things down for the world to see, such as in this weblog or carefully for Twitter, you get the benefit of fast feedback. People can see what you are interested in, and you can reach out to them with something relevant on the front page of what they see when they look for you. If there are questions that are top of mind they can go to the top of the page. It's a very effective way to take advantage of introductions, and to reach out beyond people you know.

Without the ability to write things down it's easy to flounder. You end up reviewing ground that you had gone over before because you forgot it, and didn't write it down somewhere you could find it. Decisions that were made and observations that were agreed on get revisited sometimes endlessly because there's no contemporaneous record of the conclusion. Obscure details that you want to follow up on later get wiped out.

This particular project has an internal communications tool, which will also remain nameless. It's a stylized, formalized, cartoonish caricature of what people actually use in real life, and it's designed to maximize management visibility into the process rather than the utility of the process to the goal. In many ways it reminds me of other systems I've been forced into where the sole measure of progress was the easiest thing to measure, and all of your hard qualitative work gets squeezed down into a single number (number of transactions, queue depth) that bears very little resemblance to anything useful.

I've written before about systems and why they fail; that's actually part of my 140 character Twitter biography. Here the failures probably are on my part, for not understanding the project expectations before starting, and not being able to exit gracefully before the effort is over. The whole effort has given me a sour taste. Most importantly, I understand sadly that being asked not to write has been to cut off the most important part of what I can contribute to an effort, and in retrospect I should have walked out of the room when that happened.

Edward Vielmetti
