Essential components of Ann Arbor Weather Twitter

5 September 2014

Essential components of Ann Arbor Weather Twitter

Essential components of Ann Arbor Weather Twitter:

In an emergency, call 911.

911 trunk lines can go down in storms. The Ann Arbor non-emergency police number is (734) 994-2911, put it in your cell phone.

Sirens: City sirens are tested on the second Tuesdays of the month at 1 pm. A small handful of us notice this and tweet SIRENS. Generally siren activation in any quantity will prompt twitter chatter.

Hashtags: #miwx is primary, but I also watch or post to #annarbor and #skywarn.

Amateur radio: get your ham radio license (check), an inexpensive handheld 2m VHF radio (check), a decent antenna (check), and Skywarn spotter training (to do for 2015). During severe weather monitor N8DUY 145.15 Mhz, located at the corner of I-94 and M-14 in Scio Township. Someone, somewhere, should have a Broadcastify feed for a scanner listening to that channel with backup power and good solid Internet; that's a missing link right now.

Who to follow: you're going to have to figure some of that out by yourself, but the list https://twitter.com/vielmetti/lists/annarbor and https://twitter.com/vielmetti/lists/weather might be a good start.

Power outages: If you have a smart enough smart phone, get the DTE Energy mobile app. If not, add the DTE Energy number 800-477-4747 to your phone to report outages. Follow @dte_energy and use hashtag #poweroutage I guess. The DTE outage map: http://www.dteenergy.com/map/outage.html . A snapshot of the September 5, 2014 map is below after severe storms knocked out power to more than 10,000 subscribers in Ann Arbor.


Location: if you report severe weather or storm damage or a power outage, give a neighborhood name or a cross street. If you don't know every neighborhood, Arborwiki can be a good place to look them up. Find at least one other person who is geographically nearby to you and follow them - there's no where near the neighborhood density on Twitter that there is on Facebook or email, but in Ann Arbor at least the network is dense enough.

Cameras: Post your favorite weather photos or videos to your favorite social network. Note that the current structure of Twitter, Instagram, and Vine all lend themselves to real-time reporting in ways that Facebook does not, but Facebook and Youtube might reach more people over all if your post is interesting. Tag with your favorite hashtag.

Teenage Twitter: Adults and teens don't often mix on Twitter, so it's good to follow parents of teens who will relay news and give you a heads up about what's happening. As an alternative, create a Twitter list of teenagers and don't follow them but do check in when conditions warrant.

Adult Twitter: Teens and adults don't often mix on Twitter, so it's good to follow teenagers whose parents are also on Twitter who will similarly relay news.

College Twitter: Sometimes the U of Michigan seems like it's its own city within a city, but during severe weather the official @umich account will be aware of what's going on especially if there are alerts that need to go to all students.

Local government Twitter: In an emergency, call 911. The @a2gov account is not always monitored, but when it is monitored during weather it can be helpful to guide you through the maze of municipal issues to report a problem.

Real time lightning maps: Several services have real time or near real time lightning. I like the non-commercial blitzortung.org and its companion site lightningmaps.org .
