Tonight's #wjchat is about social media for long-term editorial projects

Nov 19, 2014

wjchat is a weekly Wednesday night chat session on Twitter. "wj"

in this case is short for "web journalism". Tonight's session is on using social media for long term editorial projects.

The longest long-term editorial project I'm working on that's relevant to this is Arborwiki, which is now part of the Localwiki project. It's a local encyclopedia of the Ann Arbor area, and as such it's a very useful spot to accumulate the back story for any story that you want to tell. It's an ideal place to write the little biographical sketch for someone who's in the news, to write up a brief history of important issues, or to collect public records that help you understand the ins and outs of some ongoing project. The effort started in 2005 and is still going strong.

I've gotten so accustomed to having a wiki as an outboard memory that I understand that if I don't write something down, I'm not likely to be able to find it again. That impulse is tempered by the realization that writing it down might take an appreciable amount of time, since the value of a fragment or an incomplete image is limited.

The biggest headache of any long-term editorial project is that it's very hard to get the exact right point of view expressed so that if you walk away from the thing you're working on for a year or two that you can come back to it and bring it up to date easily. The durable pages look like a "tick-tock" with a heavy emphasis on a timeline and much less in the way of explanation. If you can at least get a solid timeline nailed down, you can at least pick up the story (in non-wiki, proper story form) after leaving it and be have some good backstory to pick and choose from.
